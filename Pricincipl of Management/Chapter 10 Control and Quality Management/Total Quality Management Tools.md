You have now moved from the **classic "Old Seven" quality tools** (Pareto, Fishbone, Control Charts, etc.) to the **Strategic/Modern TQM tools**. 

If the classic tools are about *fixing defects on the factory floor*, these strategic tools are about *redesigning the entire business system* to achieve world-class excellence. Examiners love asking about these because they show you understand TQM at a macro, strategic level.

Here is your complete, exam-ready breakdown of these **5 Strategic TQM Tools: Benchmarking, Outsourcing, Speed, ISO 9000, and Statistical Control.**

---

### 1. Benchmarking (Learning from the Best)

**Meaning:** Benchmarking is the process of comparing your organization’s products, services, processes, and practices against the **"best-in-class"** companies in your industry (or even outside your industry) to identify gaps and learn how to improve.


**The Benchmarking Process (4 Steps):**

| Step | Name | What happens here? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Plan** | Decide *what* to benchmark (e.g., customer service response time) and *who* to benchmark against (e.g., Amazon or Zappos). |
| **2** | **Collect Data** | Gather information on how the "best" company achieves its performance. Visit their facilities, study their reports, or attend industry conferences. |
| **3** | **Analyze Gaps** | Compare your performance against theirs. Identify the gap. (e.g., "They respond to customer emails in 2 hours; we take 48 hours. Gap = 46 hours.") |
| **4** | **Implement & Improve** | Adopt or adapt their best practices to close the gap. Monitor the results. |

**Types of Benchmarking:**

| Type                   | Meaning                                                                                             | Example                                                                                                                          |
| :--------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Internal**           | Comparing different departments or branches *within* your own company.                              | The South branch of a bank benchmarks its customer satisfaction against the North branch.                                        |
| **Competitive**        | Comparing directly against your direct competitors.                                                 | Marriott hotels benchmark their check-in speed against Hilton.                                                                   |
| **Functional/Generic** | Comparing a specific function against the *best in any industry*, even if they are not competitors. | A hospital benchmarks its supply chain process against Amazon's logistics, even though they are completely different industries. |

**Advantages of Benchmarking:**

- Identifies your weaknesses and blind spots.
- Brings fresh, proven ideas into the organization.
- Sets realistic, ambitious performance targets.
- Creates a culture of continuous learning and humility.

### 2. Outsourcing (Focusing on Your Core)

**Meaning:** Outsourcing is the strategic practice of contracting out specific non-core business activities, functions, or processes to external, specialized third-party vendors, rather than doing them in-house.

**Think of it like:** A top chef running a Michelin-star restaurant. The chef is amazing at *cooking*, but terrible at *accounting* and *IT*. Instead of trying to do the books themselves (and making mistakes), they hire an external accounting firm to handle payroll and taxes so they can focus 100% of their energy on creating amazing food.

**The Strategic Logic (TQM Link):**

| In-House (Do it yourself) | Outsource (Let experts do it) |
| :--- | :--- |
| You control everything, but you get distracted from your core strength. | You focus *only* on what you are best at (Core Competency). |
| You must invest huge capital in infrastructure and training. | You convert fixed costs into variable costs (pay-as-you-go). |
| You have limited expertise. | You get access to world-class expertise, technology, and economies of scale. |

### 3. Speed (The Competitive Weapon)

**Meaning:** In the context of TQM, **Speed** refers to the ability of an organization to respond to customer needs, market changes, and internal processes *rapidly* without sacrificing quality. It is about reducing lead times, cycle times, and response times.

**Think of it like:** Two pizza shops. Shop A takes 45 minutes to deliver a pizza. Shop B takes 20 minutes. If both pizzas taste equally good, Shop B will win every single time because they are *faster*. In business, speed is a massive quality differentiator.

**The 4 Dimensions of Speed in TQM:**

| Dimension | What it means | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Speed of Innovation** | How quickly you can develop and launch new products. | Apple launches a new iPhone every 12 months; if they slowed to 24 months, they would lose to Samsung. |
| **2. Speed of Production (Cycle Time)** | The time taken to convert raw materials into a finished product. | Toyota uses "Just-In-Time" (JIT) manufacturing to reduce cycle time and eliminate inventory waste. |
| **3. Speed of Delivery** | How quickly you can get the product to the customer's doorstep. | Amazon Prime offers same-day/next-day delivery. |
| **4. Speed of Problem Resolution** | How fast you can respond to complaints and fix defects. | Zappos has a famous policy: "We don't care if your shoes are defective; we will send you a new pair overnight, no questions asked." |

### 4. ISO 9000 (The International Quality Standard)

**Meaning:** ISO 9000 is a globally recognized family of international standards for **Quality Management Systems (QMS)**. It provides a framework and set of guidelines that help organizations ensure that their products and services consistently meet customer and regulatory requirements.

**Think of it like:** A driver's license. If a car repair shop is "ISO 9000 certified," it means an independent, international body has audited their processes and confirmed that they are following a strict, documented, and effective quality management system. It gives customers instant trust.

**The Core Principles of ISO 9000 (Not just one standard, but a framework):**

| Principle                             | Explanation                                                     |
| :------------------------------------ | :-------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Customer Focus**                 | Everything must revolve around meeting customer expectations.   |
| **2. Leadership**                     | Top management must be actively involved in quality.            |
| **3. Engagement of People**           | All employees must be competent and empowered.                  |
| **4. Process Approach**               | Activities must be managed as interconnected processes.         |
| **5. Continuous Improvement**         | The organization must constantly seek to improve.               |
| **6. Evidence-Based Decision Making** | Use data, not gut feelings.                                     |
| **7. Relationship Management**        | Build strong, mutually beneficial relationships with suppliers. |

**Disadvantages / Criticisms:**

| Disadvantage                   | Explanation                                                                                                                                                                    |
| :----------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Bureaucratic & Rigid**       | Can become a "tick-box" exercise where companies focus on paperwork rather than actual quality improvement.                                                                    |
| **Expensive & Time-Consuming** | Getting certified requires significant investment in documentation and audits.                                                                                                 |
| **Does not guarantee quality** | Being ISO certified does NOT mean the product is perfect; it just means the *process* is documented. You can have a perfectly documented process for making terrible products! |

### 5. Statistical Process Control (SPC)
note: spc is plotted in a graph

_"SPC is just a quantifier of what to look at and what not to look at in management."_

- **"What to look at"** = The _Critical Points_ (the key measurements that actually matter).
- **"What not to look at"** = The _Normal Random Noise_ (the tiny, natural fluctuations that are a waste of time to investigate).

_"SPC is a statistical tool that acts as a **'traffic light'** for managers. It quantifies the normal, acceptable variation in a process (the 'Green Zone') and clearly flags the abnormal, unacceptable variation (the 'Red Zone'). By doing this, it tells managers **exactly what to investigate** (the special causes) and **what to ignore** (the common causes). This prevents managers from wasting time and resources chasing 'ghosts'—random fluctuations that don't actually indicate a real problem."_

**Meaning:** Statistical Process Control (SPC) is a scientific, data-driven method of monitoring and controlling the quality of a production process by using **statistical techniques**. It involves constantly measuring the process, tracking variations, and ensuring that the process operates within defined **control limits**.

**Think of it like:** Your blood pressure monitor. It doesn't just check your BP once a year; it gives you a continuous reading. If your BP spikes into the "danger zone" (Upper Control Limit), the machine alarms, and you take immediate action before you have a heart attack.

**The Core Concepts of SPC:**

| Concept                               | Explanation                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| :------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **1. Variation**                      | No two products are ever exactly identical. Variation is natural (Common Cause) or abnormal (Special Cause). SPC helps distinguish between the two.                                                                             |
| **2. Control Charts**                 | The primary tool of SPC. A graph with a **Central Line** (the average), an **Upper Control Limit (UCL)** , and a **Lower Control Limit (LCL)** . As long as data points fall between UCL and LCL, the process is "in control."  |
| **3. Common Cause vs. Special Cause** | *Common Cause:* Inherent, random variations (e.g., temperature fluctuations in a room). <br> *Special Cause:* An external, specific event (e.g., a machine suddenly breaking down). SPC aims to eliminate Special Causes first. |

**The SPC Process (How it works):**

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Select the process** to monitor (e.g., the temperature of a baking oven). |
| **2** | **Take random samples** at regular intervals (e.g., measure the temperature every 15 minutes). |
| **3** | **Plot the data** on a Control Chart. |
| **4** | **Analyze the chart:** If points fall outside the UCL/LCL, the process is "out of control." |
| **5** | **Investigate & Correct:** Find the root cause of the deviation (e.g., a faulty thermostat) and fix it. |

**Advantages of SPC:**

| Advantage | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Proactive (Prevents defects)** | You catch problems *before* they produce defective products. |
| **Reduces Inspection Costs** | You don't need to inspect every single product; you only monitor the process. |
| **Objective Decision-Making** | Decisions are based on hard data, not emotions or guesses. |
| **Identifies Root Causes** | Helps you distinguish between random variation and serious problems. |
