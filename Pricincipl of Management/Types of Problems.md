## Classification 1: On the Basis of Frequency

This classification looks at **how often** the problem occurs.

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Routine Problems** | Occur regularly, are repetitive, and have standard solutions. | Daily operational issues. |
| **Exceptional Problems** | Occur rarely, are unique, and require special attention. | One-time crises or strategic issues. |

---

### A. Routine Problems

**Definition:** Problems that happen **frequently** and are **predictable**. They have **standard solutions** (policies, procedures, rules).

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Frequency** | Happens daily, weekly, or monthly. |
| **Predictability** | Can anticipate when they will occur. |
| **Solution** | Standard, pre-defined (programmed decisions). |
| **Who handles** | Lower management or automated systems. |
| **Risk** | Low. |

**Examples of Routine Problems:**

| Situation | Routine Problem | Standard Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Office supplies | Printer runs out of paper. | Refill paper tray. |
| Inventory | Stock reaches reorder point. | Automatic purchase order. |
| Employees | Employee is late to work. | Follow attendance policy. |
| Customers | Customer requests a refund. | Follow return policy. |
| Equipment | Machine requires daily cleaning. | Follow maintenance schedule. |

---

### B. Exceptional Problems

**Definition:** Problems that occur **rarely** and are **unpredictable**. They have **no standard solution** and require special analysis.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Frequency** | Happens once or very few times. |
| **Predictability** | Cannot anticipate (surprise events). |
| **Solution** | Non-standard, requires creativity (non-programmed decisions). |
| **Who handles** | Top or middle management. |
| **Risk** | High. |

**Examples of Exceptional Problems:**

| Situation | Exceptional Problem | Required Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Crisis | Factory catches fire. | Emergency response, investigation, recovery. |
| Competition | A major new competitor enters the market. | Strategic analysis, new strategy formulation. |
| Technology | A disruptive technology makes your product obsolete. | R&D, pivot business model. |
| Legal | New government regulation changes industry rules. | Legal analysis, compliance plan. |
| Reputation | A scandal damages brand image. | PR crisis management. |

---

### Comparison: Routine vs. Exceptional Problems

| Basis | Routine Problems | Exceptional Problems |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Frequency** | Frequent (daily, weekly) | Rare (once or few times) |
| **Predictability** | Predictable | Unpredictable |
| **Solution** | Standard (policy, procedure) | Custom (requires analysis) |
| **Decision type** | Programmed | Non-programmed |
| **Management level** | Lower management | Top/middle management |
| **Risk** | Low | High |
| **Example** | Printer out of paper | Factory fire |

## Classification 2: On the Basis of Timeframe

This classification looks at **how long** the problem takes to solve or **how far into the future** the problem extends.

| Type | Time Horizon | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Short-term problems** | Days to weeks | Can be solved quickly with immediate action. | Machine breakdown, customer complaint. |
| **Medium-term problems** | Months to 1 year | Require sustained effort over several months. | Seasonal sales decline, employee training gaps. |
| **Long-term problems** | 1 year to 5+ years | Require strategic planning and years of effort. | Declining market share, aging workforce. |

---

### A. Short-term Problems

**Definition:** Problems that can be **resolved quickly** (within days or weeks) with immediate action.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time to solve** | Hours, days, or weeks. |
| **Urgency** | Often high (needs immediate attention). |
| **Resources needed** | Small. |
| **Impact** | Usually limited. |

**Examples:**

| Problem | Solution Timeframe |
| :--- | :--- |
| Machine stops working | Fix within hours. |
| Customer complaint | Resolve within 1 day. |
| Employee absent | Find replacement for the shift. |
| Out of stock item | Reorder immediately. |

---

### B. Medium-term Problems

**Definition:** Problems that take **several months to a year** to resolve.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time to solve** | Weeks to months (up to 1 year). |
| **Urgency** | Moderate. |
| **Resources needed** | Moderate. |
| **Impact** | Significant. |

**Examples:**

| Problem | Solution Timeframe |
| :--- | :--- |
| Seasonal sales decline | Develop and execute a quarterly marketing plan. |
| Employee skill gaps | Implement a 3-month training program. |
| Low customer satisfaction | Run a 6-month service improvement initiative. |
| Production inefficiency | Implement process improvements over 4 months. |

---

### C. Long-term Problems

**Definition:** Problems that take **a year or more** to resolve and often require strategic change.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time to solve** | 1 to 5+ years. |
| **Urgency** | Low to moderate (but important). |
| **Resources needed** | Large. |
| **Impact** | Organization-wide. |

**Examples:**

| Problem | Solution Timeframe |
| :--- | :--- |
| Declining market share | 3-year strategic turnaround plan. |
| Aging technology | 5-year technology upgrade roadmap. |
| Poor company culture | 2-3 year culture transformation. |
| Loss of competitive advantage | Long-term innovation and R&D strategy. |

---

### Comparison: Short, Medium, and Long-term Problems

| Basis | Short-term | Medium-term | Long-term |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Time to solve** | Hours to weeks | Weeks to 1 year | 1 to 5+ years |
| **Urgency** | High | Moderate | Low (but important) |
| **Resources** | Small | Moderate | Large |
| **Management level** | Lower | Middle | Top |
| **Example** | Machine breakdown | Seasonal sales dip | Declining market share |

## Classification 3: On the Basis of Impact

This classification looks at **how much** of the organization is affected by the problem.

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Partial Impact Problem** | Affects only a part (department, location, product line) of the organization. | One machine breaks; one product fails; one branch underperforms. |
| **Overall Impact Problem** | Affects the entire organization or most of it. | Company-wide sales decline; reputational crisis; leadership failure. |

---

### A. Partial Impact Problem

**Definition:** The problem is **limited** to a specific department, location, product, or process. Other parts of the organization remain unaffected.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Scope** | Limited to one area. |
| **Containment** | Easy to isolate. |
| **Resources needed** | Local resources can solve it. |
| **Management level** | Lower or middle management. |

**Examples:**

| Problem | Impact Scope |
| :--- | :--- |
| Marketing department misses its quarterly target. | Only Marketing is affected. |
| One machine stops working on the production line. | Only that workstation is affected. |
| A single branch office underperforms. | Only that branch is affected. |
| One product has quality issues. | Only that product line is affected. |

---

### B. Overall Impact Problem

**Definition:** The problem affects the **entire organization** or a majority of its operations.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Scope** | Organization-wide or most areas. |
| **Containment** | Difficult to isolate; spreads across functions. |
| **Resources needed** | Significant resources from across the organization. |
| **Management level** | Top management. |

**Examples:**

| Problem | Impact Scope |
| :--- | :--- |
| Overall sales decline across all products. | Entire organization. |
| Company reputation is damaged (scandal). | All departments, all products. |
| Economic recession affects all business lines. | Entire organization. |
| CEO resigns unexpectedly. | Entire organization. |

---

### Comparison: Partial vs. Overall Impact Problems

| Basis | Partial Impact | Overall Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scope** | One department, product, or location | Entire organization |
| **Containment** | Easy to isolate | Difficult to isolate |
| **Resources** | Local resources | Organization-wide resources |
| **Management level** | Lower/middle management | Top management |
| **Example** | One machine breaks | Company-wide sales decline |

## Classification 4: On the Basis of Urgency

This classification looks at **how quickly** the problem needs to be solved.

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Urgent Problems** | Require immediate action; delay causes serious consequences. | Machine fire, product recall, safety issue. |
| **Non-Urgent Problems** | Important but can wait; can be scheduled for later action. | Gradual sales decline, outdated software. |

---

### A. Urgent Problems

**Definition:** Problems that demand **immediate attention** because delay will cause serious damage (financial, reputational, safety).

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time sensitivity** | Must be solved now (hours or days). |
| **Consequence of delay** | Severe (loss, injury, damage). |
| **Decision approach** | Often intuitive or heuristic (no time for analysis). |
| **Stress level** | High. |

**Examples of Urgent Problems:**

| Problem | Why Urgent |
| :--- | :--- |
| Factory fire | Immediate danger to life and property. |
| Product defect causing injuries | Must recall immediately to prevent harm. |
| Server crash (e-commerce) | Company losing sales every minute. |
| PR scandal on social media | Reputation damage spreads rapidly. |
| Safety violation | Immediate risk of accident or fine. |

---

### B. Non-Urgent Problems

**Definition:** Problems that are **important** but **not time-sensitive**. They can be scheduled and solved systematically.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Time sensitivity** | Can wait (weeks or months). |
| **Consequence of delay** | Moderate or low (gradual worsening). |
| **Decision approach** | Rational, analytical. |
| **Stress level** | Low to moderate. |

**Examples of Non-Urgent Problems:**

| Problem | Why Not Urgent |
| :--- | :--- |
| Gradual loss of market share | Can analyze over weeks; not immediate crisis. |
| Outdated technology | Can plan a 6-month upgrade roadmap. |
| Low employee morale | Can address through systematic HR initiatives. |
| Rising costs | Can study and implement gradual cost controls. |

---

### The Urgent vs. Important Matrix (Eisenhower Matrix)

This famous matrix helps managers prioritize problems:

| | **Urgent** | **Not Urgent** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Important** | **DO FIRST** (Crises, deadlines) | **SCHEDULE** (Planning, improvement) |
| **Not Important** | **DELEGATE** (Interruptions, some emails) | **ELIMINATE** (Time-wasters) |

| Quadrant | Problem Type | Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Important + Urgent | Crisis, fire, emergency | Do immediately. |
| Important + Not Urgent | Strategic planning, skill development | Schedule for later. |
| Not Important + Urgent | Some phone calls, minor requests | Delegate. |
| Not Important + Not Urgent | Trivial tasks | Eliminate. |

## Classification 5: On the Basis of Source

This classification looks at **where** the problem comes from. This is very practical because the source determines the solution approach.

| Type | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Technical Problem** | Caused by machines, equipment, technology, or processes. | Machine breakdown, software bug, poor quality. |
| **Human Problem** | Caused by people's behavior, attitudes, skills, or relationships. | Low morale, conflict, poor performance, absenteeism. |
| **Environmental Problem** | Caused by external factors outside the organization's control. | Economic recession, new law, natural disaster. |

---

### A. Technical Problem

**Definition:** Problems that originate from **machines, equipment, technology, systems, or processes**. They are usually objective and have clear solutions.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Physical assets, technology, processes. |
| **Nature** | Objective, measurable. |
| **Solution** | Often technical (repair, replace, redesign). |
| **Who solves** | Engineers, technicians, IT staff. |

**Examples of Technical Problems:**

| Problem | Source | Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Machine stops working | Mechanical failure | Repair or replace parts. |
| Software crashes | Code bug | Fix the code. |
| Poor product quality | Manufacturing process | Adjust process parameters. |
| Slow internet | Network issue | Upgrade bandwidth or hardware. |
| Raw material defect | Supplier quality | Change supplier or inspection process. |

---

### B. Human Problem

**Definition:** Problems that originate from **people**—their behavior, attitudes, skills, motivation, or relationships.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Employees, managers, customers, stakeholders. |
| **Nature** | Subjective, emotional, behavioral. |
| **Solution** | Training, motivation, communication, leadership. |
| **Who solves** | HR managers, team leaders, senior managers. |

**Examples of Human Problems:**

| Problem | Source | Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Low employee morale | Poor management, lack of recognition | Improve leadership, recognition programs. |
| Conflict between team members | Personality clash, miscommunication | Mediation, team building. |
| High absenteeism | Low motivation, personal issues | Counseling, flexible policies. |
| Poor customer service | Untrained or unmotivated staff | Training, incentives. |
| Resistance to change | Fear, lack of understanding | Communication, involvement. |

---

### C. Environmental Problem

**Definition:** Problems that originate from **external factors** outside the organization's control.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Economy, government, nature, competitors, society. |
| **Nature** | Uncontrollable, unpredictable. |
| **Solution** | Adaptation, response, mitigation (cannot eliminate). |
| **Who solves** | Top management, strategists. |

**Examples of Environmental Problems:**

| Problem | Source | Solution |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Economic recession | Economy | Cut costs, diversify, wait it out. |
| New government regulation | Government | Comply, adapt operations. |
| New competitor enters | Competition | Improve quality, lower prices, differentiate. |
| Natural disaster | Nature | Disaster recovery plan, insurance. |
| Changing customer tastes | Society | Change products, rebrand. |

---

### Comparison: Technical vs. Human vs. Environmental Problems

| Basis | Technical | Human | Environmental |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Machines, technology, processes | People (behavior, attitude, skill) | External factors (economy, govt, nature) |
| **Control** | Controllable | Partially controllable | Uncontrollable |
| **Nature** | Objective, measurable | Subjective, emotional | Unpredictable |
| **Solution** | Repair, replace, redesign | Training, motivation, leadership | Adapt, respond, mitigate |
| **Who solves** | Engineers, technicians | HR, managers | Top management |
| **Example** | Machine breakdown | Employee conflict | Economic recession |

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the different types of problems based on frequency, timeframe, impact, urgency, and source.

**A:**

**Definition of a Problem:** A problem is a gap between the current state and the desired state.

**Types of Problems:**

**1. On the Basis of Frequency:**
- **Routine Problems:** Occur frequently, are predictable, and have standard solutions. *Example:* Printer out of paper (solution: refill paper).
- **Exceptional Problems:** Occur rarely, are unpredictable, and require custom solutions. *Example:* Factory fire (requires emergency response).

**2. On the Basis of Timeframe:**
- **Short-term Problems:** Can be solved within days or weeks. *Example:* Machine breakdown.
- **Medium-term Problems:** Take months to a year to solve. *Example:* Seasonal sales decline.
- **Long-term Problems:** Take 1 to 5+ years to solve. *Example:* Declining market share.

**3. On the Basis of Impact:**
- **Partial Impact Problems:** Affect only one department, product, or location. *Example:* Marketing department misses its target.
- **Overall Impact Problems:** Affect the entire organization. *Example:* Company-wide sales decline.

**4. On the Basis of Urgency:**
- **Urgent Problems:** Require immediate action; delay causes serious consequences. *Example:* Product recall due to safety issue.
- **Non-Urgent Problems:** Can wait and be scheduled for later. *Example:* Gradual loss of market share.

**5. On the Basis of Source:**
- **Technical Problems:** Caused by machines, technology, or processes. *Example:* Machine breakdown (solution: repair).
- **Human Problems:** Caused by people's behavior, attitudes, or skills. *Example:* Low employee morale (solution: improve leadership).
- **Environmental Problems:** Caused by external factors outside control. *Example:* Economic recession (solution: adapt and respond).

**Conclusion:** Effective problem solvers first classify the problem by these five criteria. The classification determines the appropriate solution approach, management level, and resources needed.
