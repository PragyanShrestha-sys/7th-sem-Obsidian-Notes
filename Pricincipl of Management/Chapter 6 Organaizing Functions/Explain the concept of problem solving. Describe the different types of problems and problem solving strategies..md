**Concept of Problem Solving:**

Problem solving is the process of identifying a gap between the current state and the desired state, finding the root cause, generating and implementing solutions, and evaluating the results. It is broader than decision making and includes decision making as one of its steps.

**Types of Problems:**

1.  **Based on Structure:**
    - **Well-structured problems:** Clear, routine, with known solutions (e.g., printer out of paper).
    - **Ill-structured problems:** Vague, complex, with no clear solution (e.g., declining market share).

2.  **Based on Urgency:**
    - **Crisis problems:** Sudden, urgent, high-impact (e.g., factory fire).
    - **Non-crisis problems:** Important but not urgent (e.g., gradual sales decline).

3.  **Based on Complexity:**
    - **Simple problems:** One cause, one solution (e.g., light bulb burned out).
    - **Complex problems:** Multiple causes, multiple solutions (e.g., low employee morale).

**Problem Solving Strategies:**

1.  **Algorithm:** A step-by-step procedure that guarantees a solution. Best for well-structured problems (e.g., printer troubleshooting guide).

2.  **Heuristic:** A "rule of thumb" based on experience. Fast but not guaranteed (e.g., "When in doubt, ask your supervisor").

3.  **Trial and Error:** Trying different solutions until one works. Best for simple, low-risk problems.

4.  **Brainstorming:** Group generates many creative ideas without criticism. Best for ill-structured problems needing innovation.

5.  **Root Cause Analysis:** Finding the underlying cause, not just symptoms. Uses techniques like the "5 Whys." Best for recurring problems.

6.  **Divide and Conquer:** Breaking a large problem into smaller sub-problems. Best for large, complex problems.

7.  **Means-Ends Analysis:** Comparing current state to goal and taking steps to reduce the gap. Best for planning and goal-oriented problems.

8.  **Analogical Reasoning:** Using solutions from similar past problems. Best for familiar problems in new contexts.

**Conclusion:** Effective problem solvers first classify the type of problem they face, then select the appropriate strategy. Different problems require different approaches.
