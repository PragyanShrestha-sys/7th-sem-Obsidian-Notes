### 1. The Classical Approach (The "Machine" View)

**Timeframe:** Early 1900s (Pioneers: Frederick Taylor, Henri Fayol, Max Weber)
**Core Metaphor:** **The Organization is a Machine.**

- **What it believes:** Workers are primarily motivated by money and physical needs. They are rational economic beings. Therefore, organizing is a purely **logical and scientific** process. 
- **How they organize:** The **contingency approach** (or situational approach) ==dictates that there is no single best way to manage an organization==. Instead, the most effective management style, leadership approach, or organizational structure is fluid. It is entirely dependent upon aligning internal capabilities with external situational variables and environmental needs.

**Key Features of this Approach:**

- **High Formalization:** Everything is written down—strict job descriptions, detailed rules, and standard operating procedures.
- **Centralized Authority:** Decision-making power sits strictly at the top. Middle and lower managers just *execute* orders.
- **Narrow Span of Control:** Managers supervise very few people to maintain tight control.
- **Strict Unity of Command:** One boss, one employee. No exceptions.
- **Departmentalization:** Work is divided into clear, separate silos (e.g., Purchasing, Sales, Finance) that don't interfere with each other.

**Strengths:** Highly efficient, predictable, and productive. Great for routine, repetitive work (like manufacturing).
**Weaknesses:** Extremely rigid. It treats humans like cogs in a machine, ignores emotions, kills creativity, and fails completely when unexpected changes happen.

### 2. The Behavioural Approach (The "Social" View)

**Timeframe:** 1930s–1960s (Pioneers: Elton Mayo, Douglas McGregor, Chester Barnard)
**Core Metaphor:** **The Organization is a Community/Family.**

- **What it believes:** Workers are not just motivated by money; they are social and emotional beings. They need recognition, belonging, and participation. (This was discovered accidentally in the famous **Hawthorne Studies**, where workers produced more simply because researchers were *paying attention* to them!).
- **How they organize:** They believe organizing should focus on **human relationships** and informal groups, rather than just rigid structures. 

**Key Features of this Approach:**

- **Informal Organization:** Alongside the official "org chart," there are informal groups (friendships, coffee cliques). Good managers recognize these and use them to boost morale.
- **Decentralized Authority:** Managers should delegate power to lower levels because employees perform better when they have autonomy and a say in decisions.
- **Wider Span of Control:** Since employees are self-motivated, a manager can supervise more people without needing to micromanage.
- **Two-Way Communication:** Instead of just orders flowing *down*, managers must listen to feedback flowing *up* from workers.
- **Participative Decision-Making:** Involve workers in decisions that affect their jobs (e.g., "How should we rearrange this workshop?").

**Strengths:** Boosts employee morale, job satisfaction, and loyalty. Encourages innovation and teamwork.
**Weaknesses:** Can be slow to make decisions (too many meetings). Ignores that some workers *prefer* clear, strict orders and don't want the pressure of decision-making. Also assumes happy workers are always productive workers (which isn't always true).

---

### 3. The Contingency Approach (The "Chameleon" View)

**Timeframe:** 1960s–Present (Pioneers: Paul Lawrence, Jay Lorsch, Fred Fiedler)
**Core Metaphor:** **The Organization is a Living Organism adapting to its environment.**

- **What it believes:** **THERE IS NO ONE BEST WAY** to organize! The "best" structure depends entirely on the *situation*—the company's size, technology, industry, and the outside environment (economy, competition, government).
- **How they organize:** Instead of following a rulebook, managers must analyze their specific **external environment**. The structure must "fit" the context.

**Key Features of this Approach:**

- **Environment Dictates Structure:** 
  - If the environment is **stable and predictable** (e.g., a government post office), a *Classical* rigid structure works best.
  - If the environment is **dynamic and uncertain** (e.g., a tech startup in AI), a *Behavioural* flexible, flat structure works best.
- **No Universal Principles:** Principles like "Unity of Command" or "Span of Control" are just *suggestions*. If breaking them helps the company survive, you break them!
- **Organic vs. Mechanistic Structures:** 
  - **Mechanistic** (Classic): Rigid, hierarchical, for stable environments.
  - **Organic** (Behavioural): Flexible, team-based, for changing environments.
- **"It Depends" Management:** A manager's organizing style must match the situation. If you have a team of highly educated professionals, give them freedom. If you have a team of untrained temporary workers, give them strict rules.

**Strengths:** Realistic, practical, and adaptive. It prevents managers from blindly following outdated textbooks.
**Weaknesses:** Can be confusing. It doesn't give a clear "right answer" to a manager; it requires high-level analytical skills to figure out which approach to use.

### Quick Comparison Table (Exam Gold!)

| Feature             | Classical Approach          | Behavioural Approach           | Contingency Approach           |
| :------------------ | :-------------------------- | :----------------------------- | :----------------------------- |
| **Focus**           | Structure & Rules           | People & Relationships         | Situation & Environment        |
| **View of Worker**  | Rational & Economic         | Social & Emotional             | Variable (depends on context)  |
| **Decision-Making** | Centralized (Top-down)      | Decentralized (Participative)  | Depends on the environment     |
| **Structure**       | Rigid, Mechanistic          | Flexible, Organic              | Either, depending on the "fit" |
| **Motto**           | "One best way to organize." | "Happy workers = Better work." | "It all depends!"              |

### The Best Way to Answer an Exam Question on This:

If a question asks: *"Compare the approaches to organizing,"* **do not** just list them. Show the examiner you see the **evolution**:

> *"The Classical approach laid the foundation by focusing on structure and efficiency. However, it treated humans as machines, which led to the Behavioural approach that emphasized human needs and informal groups. Finally, the Contingency approach combined both by arguing that a manager must analyze the external environment and choose a mix of Classical and Behavioural elements that best fits their specific situation."*
