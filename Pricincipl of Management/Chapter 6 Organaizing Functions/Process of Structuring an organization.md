Perfect! Now we are getting into the **actual "how-to"** of organizing. 

If the *approaches* (Classical, Behavioural, Contingency) are the **philosophies** behind organizing, the **process of structuring** is the **step-by-step blueprint** you follow to build the actual organization.

In management textbooks, the process of structuring an organization is typically broken down into **6 logical steps**. Think of it like building a house: you wouldn't just pile up bricks; you need a systematic process (foundation → pillars → walls → roof). 

Here is the step-by-step process of structuring an organization:

---

### Step 1: Determination of Objectives
*(Deciding what house to build)*

- **What it is:** Before you draw a single box on an organizational chart, you must be crystal clear about the organization's goals. What are we trying to achieve? (e.g., Increase market share by 20%, launch a new mobile app, or reduce production costs by 10%).
- **Why it matters:** The structure is purely a *tool* to achieve objectives. If your goal changes, your structure must change. If your goal is cost-cutting, you will create a rigid, centralized structure. If your goal is innovation, you will create a loose, flexible structure.

---

### Step 2: Identification and Enumeration of Activities
*(Listing all the bricks and materials needed)*

- **What it is:** Once you know your goal, you must list *everything* that needs to be done to achieve it. You break the big goal into smaller, specific tasks and activities. 
- **Example:** If your goal is to "manufacture and sell shoes," your activities include: sourcing leather, cutting fabric, stitching, quality checking, designing advertisements, managing salaries, maintaining machinery, etc.
- **Why it matters:** If you miss a single activity at this stage (e.g., you forget "quality checking"), your entire structure will have a gap, and the organization will fail.

---

### Step 3: Grouping of Activities (Departmentalization)
*(Arranging the bricks into rooms/divisions)*

- **What it is:** Now, you take all those individual activities and group them together based on similarity, function, or purpose. This creates **departments**.
- **Common ways to group:** 
  - By **Function** (Production Dept., Marketing Dept., Finance Dept., HR Dept.)
  - By **Product** (Smartphone Division, Laptop Division, Tablet Division)
  - By **Geography** (North America Region, Europe Region, Asia Region)
  - By **Customer** (Retail Division, Corporate Division, Government Division)
- **Why it matters:** Grouping creates synergy. When all marketing experts sit together in one department, they share ideas, resources, and expertise, avoiding duplication of work.

---

### Step 4: Assignment of Duties (Allocation of Work)
*(Assigning each brick to a specific worker)*

- **What it is:** After creating departments, you assign specific tasks from Step 2 to specific individuals within those departments. This is where you write **Job Descriptions**.
- **Key rule:** The job assigned must match the employee's skills, qualifications, and experience. 
- **Example:** You don't assign a creative graphic designer to do the company's tax accounting. You assign the right person to the right task.
- **Why it matters:** Clear duty assignment eliminates confusion, overlaps, and "passing the buck" (where everyone assumes someone else is doing the work).

---

### Step 5: Delegation of Authority
*(Giving the worker the permission and power to lay that brick)*

- **What it is:** Assigning duties is useless unless you give that person the **authority** (the right to make decisions and use resources) to actually complete the task. This step involves three sub-elements:
  - **Granting Authority:** Giving them the power to command subordinates and spend money.
  - **Creating Responsibility:** Making them accountable for the outcomes.
  - **Establishing Accountability:** Making it clear that they must answer to their superior for the results.
- **Why it matters:** If you give a manager the *responsibility* to launch a new product but give them *zero authority* to approve the budget, they will fail. Authority must equal responsibility.

---

### Step 6: Establishing Reporting Relationships (Coordination & Hierarchy)
*(Connecting all the rooms with hallways and doors)*

- **What it is:** This is the final step where you link all the departments and individuals together. You clearly define **who reports to whom** and establish the formal lines of communication. This is when you actually draw the **Organizational Chart** (the pyramid structure).
- **This step finalizes:**
  - **The Span of Control:** How many people will a manager oversee?
  - **The Scalar Chain:** The clear line of authority from the CEO down to the lowest-level worker.
  - **Coordination Mechanisms:** How will the Production department communicate with the Sales department? (e.g., daily meetings, formal reports, liaison officers).
- **Why it matters:** Without this, you have isolated departments working in silos. The Sales team might promise a delivery date that the Production team cannot physically meet, leading to chaos and lost customers.

---

### The Complete Process in a Nutshell (Exam Flowchart):

> **Objectives** → **Activities** → **Group into Departments** → **Assign to People** → **Give Authority** → **Connect them all.**

---

### A Real-World Example to Lock it In:

Imagine you are starting a **Small Bakery Business**.

1. **Objective:** To sell 500 fresh loaves of bread and 200 pastries daily in your local town.
2. **Activities:** Baking bread, baking pastries, buying flour/eggs, managing cash, serving customers, cleaning, advertising on social media.
3. **Grouping:** You create 3 departments—**Production** (bakers), **Sales** (cashiers & counter staff), and **Admin/Procurement** (buying supplies & managing money).
4. **Assignment:** You assign Rahul to manage Production, Priya to manage Sales, and Anil to manage Admin. 
5. **Delegation:** You give Rahul the authority to hire 2 assistant bakers and buy up to $500 worth of ingredients without asking your permission each time.
6. **Establishing Relationships:** You tell the Sales team they must check daily production numbers with Rahul *before* promising custom cake orders to customers. You draw an org chart showing that Rahul, Priya, and Anil all directly report to *you* (the owner).
