Here is a detailed explanation of the five changing perspectives on organizations, structured to cover **what** each perspective is, **why** it emerged (the shift from previous views), and **how** it conceptualizes an organization.

---

### i) Mechanistic Viewpoint on Organization

- **What it is:**  
  Views the organization as a **closed, rational machine**. It is designed for maximum efficiency, predictability, and control. Structure is rigid, with clear hierarchies, specialized tasks, standardized rules, and unity of command. Employees are seen as interchangeable parts motivated primarily by economic rewards.

- **Why it emerged (the shift from earlier, pre-classical views):**  
  This perspective arose during the Industrial Revolution to solve problems of chaos, inconsistency, and low productivity. Prior to this, work was often informal or craft-based. The mechanistic view provided a scientific, logical way to manage large factories and bureaucracies, replacing tradition with systematic efficiency.

- **How it works (key mechanisms):**  
  - **Division of labor** into small, repeatable tasks (Taylor’s Scientific Management).  
  - **Scalar chain & centralization** (Fayol) – authority flows top-down.  
  - **Bureaucratic rules** (Weber) – decisions follow written policies.  
  - **Control through supervision** and performance standards.  
  - **Rewards/punishments** based on meeting output quotas.

> **Example:** An assembly line where each worker performs one specific action, supervised by a manager who enforces strict time and motion rules.

---

### ii) Organization as an Open System

- **What it is:**  
  Views the organization as a **living organism** that constantly interacts with its external environment. It takes in inputs (raw materials, information, people), transforms them through internal processes, and produces outputs (goods, services, results), with feedback loops guiding adaptation.

- **Why it emerged:**  
  The mechanistic view failed in dynamic, uncertain environments. Organizations were experiencing rapid market changes, technological shifts, and social pressures. The open system perspective recognized that survival depends on adapting to external conditions, not just internal efficiency.

- **How it works:**  
  - **Input → Throughput → Output → Feedback** cycle.  
  - **Subsystems** (production, maintenance, adaptive, managerial) are interdependent.  
  - **Equifinality** – multiple ways to achieve the same goal.  
  - **Negative entropy** – importing resources to avoid decay.  
  - Continuous monitoring of the environment (customers, competitors, laws, technology).

> **Example:** A retail company adjusting inventory based on customer demand data (feedback), supplier changes (input), and competitor pricing (environmental pressure).

---

### iii) Organization as a Political System

- **What it is:**  
  Views the organization as an **arena of power, conflict, and negotiation**. Different individuals and groups have divergent interests, values, and goals. Formal authority is only one source of power; informal influence, coalitions, bargaining, and politics drive most decisions.

- **Why it emerged:**  
  The open system perspective assumed harmony and shared goals (like an organism working for survival). In reality, internal conflicts, resource scrambles, and hidden agendas were common. This perspective emerged from critical management studies and real-world observation that not everyone wants the same thing.

- **How it works:**  
  - **Scarce resources** (budget, promotions, information) create competition.  
  - **Coalitions** form around shared interests, even across formal hierarchies.  
  - **Power tactics** – lobbying, withholding information, alliances, controlling agendas.  
  - **Decisions** are often outcomes of negotiation, not rational planning.  
  - Conflict is managed, not eliminated; politics is seen as natural.

> **Example:** Two department heads lobbying executives for the same budget, forming temporary alliances with other managers, and using data selectively to influence the final decision.

---

### iv) Organization as Culture

- **What it is:**  
  Views the organization as a **small society** with its own shared values, beliefs, rituals, stories, symbols, and language. Culture is the “glue” that shapes how members think, feel, and act – often more powerfully than formal rules.

- **Why it emerged:**  
  The political system perspective explained conflict well but ignored shared meaning and unity. Researchers found that successful companies had strong, distinctive cultures that motivated employees beyond rewards or power. This perspective arose from anthropology applied to business (e.g., Peters & Waterman, Deal & Kennedy).

- **How it works:**  
  - **Artifacts** (office layout, dress codes, ceremonies) visible layer.  
  - **Espoused values** (mission statements, “customer first”).  
  - **Basic underlying assumptions** (unspoken beliefs like “people are lazy” or “innovation is risky”).  
  - **Socialization** – new members learn culture through stories and mentoring.  
  - Culture guides behavior without constant supervision; “how we do things around here.”

> **Example:** A tech startup with open offices, hackathons (rituals), a legend about the founder coding overnight (story), and a belief that failure is learning (value).

---

### v) Organization as a Learning System

- **What it is:**  
  Views the organization as a **living brain** capable of continuous adaptation, knowledge creation, and self-improvement. It prioritizes experimentation, feedback loops, reflection, and sharing knowledge so that the organization becomes more intelligent over time.

- **Why it emerged:**  
  The culture perspective explained stability but not deliberate change. In fast-moving industries (technology, pharmaceuticals), the ability to learn faster than competitors became a key advantage. This perspective arose from systems thinking and works of Senge, Argyris, and Schön.

- **How it works:**  
  - **Single-loop learning** – correcting errors while keeping existing policies.  
  - **Double-loop learning** – questioning and changing underlying assumptions, goals, or rules.  
  - **Knowledge management** – capturing, storing, and sharing both explicit (documents) and tacit (experience) knowledge.  
  - **Feedback systems** – everyone can access data, reflect, and suggest changes.  
  - **Psychological safety** – members can experiment and fail without punishment.  
  - **Distributed intelligence** – learning happens at all levels, not just top management.

> **Example:** A hospital that conducts “mortality and morbidity” conferences openly discussing errors (double-loop learning), uses a digital database for patient outcomes (knowledge management), and allows nurses to suggest protocol changes.

---

### Summary Table: Changing Perspectives

| Perspective      | Core Metaphor | Key Focus                           | Why Shift Occurred                             |
| ---------------- | ------------- | ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Mechanistic      | Machine       | Efficiency, control, predictability | Industrial need for order over chaos           |
| Open System      | Organism      | Adaptation, environment interaction | Mechanistic failed in changing markets         |
| Political System | Arena         | Power, conflict, negotiation        | Open system ignored internal competition       |
| Culture          | Tribe/Society | Shared meaning, values, rituals     | Political system ignored unity & identity      |
| Learning System  | Brain         | Continuous adaptation, knowledge    | Culture alone couldn’t drive deliberate change |

Each new perspective did not fully discard the previous ones; rather, it **added a new lens** for understanding organizational complexity. A modern manager might see their organization as a mechanistic structure **plus** an open system adapting to markets **plus** a political arena **plus** a culture **plus** a learning system – all at the same time.
