Excellent question. You are now moving into the **dynamic and behavioral aspects** of organizational goals—what happens to goals once they are set and pursued over time.

**Goal succession** and **goal displacement** are two critical concepts that explain how goals change, evolve, or get corrupted. Understanding these helps you recognize why organizations sometimes drift from their original purpose or continue changing even after achieving their targets.

Let me explain each clearly.

---

## Part 1: Goal Succession

### Definition

**Goal succession** occurs when an organization achieves its existing goals and then replaces them with new, often higher or more challenging, goals.

It is the **orderly and expected progression** from one goal to the next. When an old goal is attained, a new one takes its place.

### Why Does Goal Succession Happen?

- **Human nature:** People and organizations are rarely satisfied with the status quo. Achievement creates aspiration for more.
- **Survival:** In competitive environments, standing still means falling behind.
- **Stakeholder expectations:** Investors, customers, and employees expect continuous improvement.
- **Resource slack:** Achieving a goal often frees up resources (money, talent, time) that can be directed toward new goals.

### Types of Goal Succession

| Type | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Horizontal succession** | New goal is similar in difficulty but different in content. | A company achieves "10% market share in India" → New goal: "10% market share in Brazil" |
| **Vertical succession** | New goal is higher or more challenging in the same domain. | A company achieves "₹100 crore revenue" → New goal: "₹200 crore revenue" |
| **Replacement succession** | Original goal becomes obsolete; a completely different goal is adopted. | A typewriter company achieves "dominate typewriter market" → Then computers make typewriters obsolete → New goal: "Become a leader in office software" |

### Positive Aspects of Goal Succession

- **Prevents complacency:** Organizations do not rest on past achievements.
- **Drives growth and innovation:** Continuous improvement becomes institutionalized.
- **Maintains employee motivation:** New challenges prevent boredom and stagnation.
- **Ensures organizational survival:** Adapting goals keeps the organization relevant.

### Example of Goal Succession

> **SpaceX (Elon Musk):**
> - **Goal 1 (achieved):** Build a rocket that reaches orbit. → (Falcon 1, 2008)
> - **Goal 2 (achieved):** Deliver cargo to the International Space Station. → (Dragon, 2012)
> - **Goal 3 (achieved):** Launch and land reusable rockets. → (Falcon 9, 2015–2016)
> - **Goal 4 (current):** Send humans to Mars.
> - **Goal 5 (future):** Establish a self-sustaining city on Mars.

Each achieved goal is succeeded by a more ambitious one.

## Part 2: Goal Displacement

### Definition

**Goal displacement** occurs when an organization becomes so focused on the *means* (methods, rules, procedures, or intermediate targets) that it loses sight of the original *ends* (the true purpose or final goal). The means become ends in themselves.

It is the **unintended and often dysfunctional** deviation from the organization's core purpose.

### How Does Goal Displacement Happen?

Goal displacement typically follows this path:

```text
Original Goal (Service to customers)
        ↓
Create rules/procedures to achieve goal (e.g., process forms within 24 hours)
        ↓
People become obsessed with following rules perfectly
        ↓
Original goal is forgotten
        ↓
Following the rule becomes the goal itself
```

### Negative Consequences of Goal Displacement

- **Loss of organizational purpose:** The reason for existence is forgotten.
- **Customer dissatisfaction:** Serving internal rules replaces serving external customers.
- **Employee frustration:** Workers see absurdity but feel powerless to change it.
- **Innovation death:** No one questions whether the rules still make sense.
- **Ethical problems:** People justify harmful actions because "I was just following procedures."

## Part 3: Key Differences Between Goal Succession and Goal Displacement

| Dimension | Goal Succession | Goal Displacement |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | Old goal replaced by a new, often higher goal after achievement. | Means (rules, metrics) become ends; original goal is forgotten. |
| **Direction of change** | Forward, progressive, often positive. | Sideways or backward, usually negative. |
| **Intentionality** | Usually intentional and planned. | Usually unintentional and unplanned. |
| **Relationship to original goal** | Builds upon or extends the original goal. | Abandons or contradicts the original goal. |
| **Effect on performance** | Generally improves performance and growth. | Generally harms performance and purpose. |
| **Manager's response** | Encourage and institutionalize. | Detect and correct. |

---


## Part 5: The Relationship Between the Two Concepts

In an organization's life cycle, these two forces operate simultaneously:

```text
Original Goal
     ↓
Goal Achieved → GOAL SUCCESSION → New, Higher Goal
     ↓
(If not careful)
     ↓
GOAL DISPLACEMENT → Rules and metrics become ends
     ↓
Crisis / Loss of Purpose
     ↓
Intervention / Restructuring to return to original mission
```

> **Real-world observation:** Many successful organizations cycle between succession (healthy growth) and displacement (bureaucratic drift). Recognizing displacement early is a key managerial skill.
