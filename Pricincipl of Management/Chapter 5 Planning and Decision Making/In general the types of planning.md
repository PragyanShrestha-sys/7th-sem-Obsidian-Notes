Excellent. You've understood the **concept of planning**. Now we move to the **types of planning**—which is a very common exam question. Managers use different types of plans depending on the **time horizon**, **frequency of use**, and **level of management**.

Here is a clear, structured explanation of the **types of planning**.

---

## Overview: The Three Main Ways to Classify Plans

| Basis of Classification | Types of Plans |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1. Time Horizon** | Long-term, Medium-term, Short-term plans |
| **2. Frequency of Use** | Single-use plans, Standing plans |
| **3. Level of Management** | Strategic, Tactical, Operational plans |

> **Note:** These classifications overlap. For example, a strategic plan is usually long-term and non-programmed. A standing plan is usually short-to-medium term and operational.

Let's explore each classification in detail.

---

## Type 1: Based on Time Horizon

This classification depends on **how far into the future** the plan looks.

| Type | Time Period | Who Makes It | Characteristics | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Long-term plans** | 3–10+ years | Top management | Broad scope, less detailed, high uncertainty, strategic focus | "Become market leader in 5 years" or "Expand to 3 new countries by 2030" |
| **Medium-term plans** | 1–3 years | Middle management | Moderate detail, connects long-term goals to short-term actions | "Launch 2 new products next year" or "Increase market share by 5% in 2 years" |
| **Short-term plans** | Up to 1 year (daily, weekly, monthly) | Lower management | High detail, specific tasks, low uncertainty | "Produce 500 units this week" or "Hire 10 staff this month" |

### Diagram: Time Horizon Pyramid

```

       /  \
      /    \
     / Long-term \  ← 3–10+ years (Vision, Direction)
    /  (Strategic) \
   /----------------\
  / Medium-term     \  ← 1–3 years (Tactical, Connecting)
 /   (Tactical)      \
/--------------------\
| Short-term         |  ← Day to 1 year (Actions, Tasks)
|  (Operational)     |
|____________________|
```

## Type 2: Based on Frequency of Use

This classification depends on **how often the plan is used**—once or repeatedly.

### A. Single-Use Plans (For One-Time Events)

These plans are created for a **specific, non-recurring situation**. Once the goal is achieved, the plan is discarded.

| Type of Single-Use Plan | Explanation | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Program/Project** | A complex set of activities to achieve a major goal | "Build a new factory" or "Launch a new software" |
| **Budget** | A plan expressed in numbers (money, time, units) | "Marketing budget of $50,000 for Q3" |
| **Schedule** | A timeline showing when each task must be done | "Construction schedule: Foundation by week 4" |

> **Example:** A company decides to host its annual conference. The event plan, venue booking, catering schedule, and budget are all **single-use plans**. After the conference, these plans are not used again.

### B. Standing Plans (For Repeated Use)

These plans are designed for **recurring situations**. They provide ongoing guidance for routine decisions. They remain in use until officially changed or canceled.

| Type of Standing Plan | Explanation | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Policy** | A general guideline for decision making (broad, flexible) | "Return policy: 30-day full refund" or "HR policy: No discrimination" |
| **Procedure** | A step-by-step sequence of actions for a routine task (rigid, specific) | "Procedure for employee onboarding" or "Steps to process a customer complaint" |
| **Rule** | A specific, rigid directive that must be followed (no discretion) | "No smoking in the office" or "Report to work by 9:00 AM sharp" |

### Comparison: Policy vs. Procedure vs. Rule (Common Exam Question)

| Aspect | Policy | Procedure | Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Flexibility** | High (managers can interpret) | Medium (steps must be followed, but some variation) | None (must be followed exactly) |
| **Purpose** | Guides thinking | Guides action | Enforces discipline |
| **Example** | "We promote from within whenever possible" | "Step 1: Post job. Step 2: Screen resumes. Step 3: Interview." | "No overtime without prior approval" |

## Type 3: Based on Level of Management

This classification links planning to **who** does the planning.

| Level of Management | Type of Plan | Time Horizon | Scope | Detail | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Top Management** (CEO, Board) | Strategic Plans | Long-term (3–10+ years) | Entire organization | Broad, vague | "Achieve 20% market share by 2030" |
| **Middle Management** (Department heads) | Tactical Plans | Medium-term (1–3 years) | Specific departments | Moderate | "Marketing department will run 4 campaigns next year" |
| **Lower Management** (Supervisors) | Operational Plans | Short-term (daily, weekly, monthly) | Specific tasks or teams | Highly detailed | "Team A will produce 200 units per day this week" |

## How Different Types Work Together (Interconnection)

Different types of plans do not exist in isolation. They form a **hierarchy**.

```
Strategic Plan (Long-term, Top Management)
        │
        ▼ (translates into)
Tactical Plans (Medium-term, Middle Management)
        │
        ▼ (translates into)
Operational Plans (Short-term, Lower Management)
        │
        ▼ (uses)
Standing Plans (Policies, Procedures, Rules) ← Guides routine actions
        AND
Single-use Plans (Budgets, Schedules, Projects) ← For specific initiatives
```

**Example:** 
- Strategic: "Expand to India" 
- → Tactical: "Increase production by 30%" 
- → Operational: "Run factory 2 extra hours daily" 
- → Standing Plan: "Overtime pay policy" 
- → Single-use Plan: "Budget for extra electricity and wages"

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the different types of planning based on time horizon and frequency of use.

**A:**

Planning is essential for every manager. Plans can be classified in different ways. The two most important classifications are based on **time horizon** and **frequency of use**.

### A. Based on Time Horizon:

1.  **Long-term plans:** Cover 3–10+ years. Made by top management. Broad, strategic, and less detailed. Example: "Expand to 5 new countries by 2030."

2.  **Medium-term plans:** Cover 1–3 years. Made by middle management. Connect long-term goals to short-term actions. Example: "Increase market share by 5% in 2 years."

3.  **Short-term plans:** Cover up to 1 year. Made by lower management. Highly detailed and specific. Example: "Produce 500 units this week."

### B. Based on Frequency of Use:

1.  **Single-use plans:** Made for one-time, non-recurring situations. Discarded after use. Examples: Project plan, budget, schedule.

2.  **Standing plans:** Used repeatedly for recurring situations. Remain in force until changed. Examples:
    - *Policy:* General guideline (e.g., "30-day return policy")
    - *Procedure:* Step-by-step method (e.g., "employee hiring steps")
    - *Rule:* Rigid directive (e.g., "No smoking")

### Conclusion:

Effective organizations use a mix of all these plans. Long-term plans provide direction, while short-term and standing plans ensure daily activities align with that direction.

**TIME** horizon → **L**ong, **M**edium, **S**hort → **LMS** (like a learning system)

**FREQUENCY** of use → **S**ingle-use, **S**tanding → **2 S's**

**LEVEL** of management → **S**trategic (Top), **T**actical (Middle), **O**perational (Lower) → **STO** (like "store" without the R)

Or remember: **TOP → MID → LOW** matches **STRATEGIC → TACTICAL → OPERATIONAL**
