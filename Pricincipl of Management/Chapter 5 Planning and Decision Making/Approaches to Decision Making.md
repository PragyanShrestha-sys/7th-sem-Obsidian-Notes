## Approach 1: Classical Approach (Rational / Economic Model)

### What is the Classical Approach?

The **Classical Approach** assumes that managers are **perfectly rational**, have **complete information**, and always make decisions that **maximize** organizational goals (profit, efficiency, etc.). It is the **ideal** or **prescriptive** model—how managers *should* make decisions.

> **In simple words:** "I will gather all information, list all options, and pick the absolute best one."

### Key Assumptions

| Assumption | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Complete information** | Manager knows all possible alternatives and all possible outcomes. |
| **Perfect rationality** | Manager is logical, objective, and consistent. |
| **Clear goal** | The objective is known and agreed upon. |
| **No time/cost constraints** | Unlimited time and resources to analyze. |
| **Maximization** | Manager chooses the alternative that gives the highest benefit (maximum profit, lowest cost). |

### The Classical Decision Making Process

```
Step 1: Identify the problem
   ↓
Step 2: Define decision criteria
   ↓
Step 3: Assign weights to criteria
   ↓
Step 4: Generate all possible alternatives
   ↓
Step 5: Evaluate each alternative
   ↓
Step 6: Select the optimal alternative (MAXIMIZE)
   ↓
Step 7: Implement
   ↓
Step 8: Evaluate outcome
```

### Example of Classical Approach

**Situation:** A company needs to choose a location for a new factory.

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Problem: Current factory is at full capacity. |
| 2 | Criteria: Cost, labor availability, taxes, proximity to customers. |
| 3 | Weights: Cost (40%), Labor (30%), Tax (20%), Proximity (10%). |
| 4 | Alternatives: City A, City B, City C, City D, City E (all possible cities). |
| 5 | Evaluate: Score each city on each criterion. |
| 6 | Select: City B has the highest total score (OPTIMAL). |
| 7 | Implement: Build factory in City B. |
| 8 | Evaluate: Check after 1 year if actual costs matched predictions. |

### When is Classical Approach Used?

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| Major strategic decisions | Mergers, acquisitions, large investments |
| When data is available | Decisions with good historical data |
| When time permits | Long-term planning, annual budgets |
| High-stakes decisions | Where mistakes are very costly |

### Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Logical and systematic | Assumes perfect information (unrealistic) |
| Produces optimal decisions | Too time-consuming for routine decisions |
| Objective and unbiased | Ignores human limitations |
| Easy to justify and defend | Real world is uncertain and complex |
| Provides clear framework | Assumes goals are clear and agreed upon |

> **Key takeaway:** The Classical Approach is an **ideal model**—something to strive for, but rarely achieved perfectly in reality.

## Approach 2: Administrative Approach (Bounded Rationality Model)

### What is the Administrative Approach?

The **Administrative Approach** was developed by **Herbert Simon** (Nobel Prize winner, 1978). He argued that managers are **not perfectly rational** because they face **limitations** (bounds). Instead of maximizing, they **satisfice**—they choose a solution that is **"good enough."**

> **In simple words:** "I cannot check every option. I will pick the first option that meets my minimum requirements."

### Key Assumptions

| Assumption | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Bounded rationality** | Managers are rational but limited by constraints. |
| **Limited information** | Managers never have complete information. |
| **Limited time** | Decisions must be made quickly. |
| **Limited cognitive ability** | Human brain cannot process unlimited data. |
| **Satisficing** | Managers accept a "good enough" solution, not the optimal one. |

### The Bounds (Limitations) on Rationality

| Bound | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Information bounds** | Cannot know everything; some data is unavailable or too expensive. |
| **Time bounds** | Decisions cannot wait forever; must act before perfect information arrives. |
| **Cognitive bounds** | Human brain can only process so much information at once. |
| **Resource bounds** | No budget for endless analysis. |
| **Environmental bounds** | The future is uncertain; outcomes cannot be known. |

### Key Concept: Satisficing vs. Maximizing

| Term | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Maximizing** (Classical) | Finding the absolute best option | Checking every house for sale in the city to find the perfect one |
| **Satisficing** (Administrative) | Finding the first option that is "good enough" | Looking at 5 houses and picking the first one that meets your basic needs (price, size, location) |

### The Administrative Decision Making Process

```
Step 1: Identify the problem (vaguely defined)
   ↓
Step 2: Set minimum acceptable criteria (aspiration level)
   ↓
Step 3: Search for alternatives (limited search)
   ↓
Step 4: Evaluate alternatives one by one
   ↓
Step 5: Stop at the FIRST alternative that meets criteria (SATISFICE)
   ↓
Step 6: Implement
   ↓
Step 7: Adjust aspiration level if no solution found
```

### Example of Administrative Approach

**Situation:** A manager needs to hire a new assistant.

| Step | Action (Classical) | Action (Administrative) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | Review all 500 resumes | Review resumes until you find 10 good ones |
| 2 | Interview all 50 qualified candidates | Interview candidates until you find one who meets criteria |
| 3 | Test top 20 candidates | Stop at first acceptable candidate |
| 4 | Select the absolute best | **Hire the first "good enough" candidate** |

**Minimum criteria (aspiration level):**
- Bachelor's degree
- 2 years experience
- Good communication skills
- Within salary budget

**First candidate who meets all four criteria = HIRED.**

### When is Administrative Approach Used?

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| Most real-world decisions | Routine, daily, operational decisions |
| When time is limited | Urgent decisions, quick responses |
| When information is costly | Small decisions where analysis would cost more than the benefit |
| When decision is not critical | Choosing office supplies, hiring entry-level staff |

### Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Realistic and practical | May miss the truly best solution |
| Saves time and effort | "Good enough" may become mediocre over time |
| Works well in routine decisions | Depends on quality of minimum standards |
| Recognizes human limitations | Not suitable for major strategic decisions |
| Can be done quickly | May lead to settling too early |

> **Key takeaway:** Most real-world decisions use the Administrative Approach (bounded rationality). Managers satisfice because they cannot maximize.

## Approach 3: Modern Approach (Behavioral / Political Model)

### What is the Modern Approach?

The **Modern Approach** (also called Behavioral or Political Model) recognizes that organizations are made up of **different individuals and groups with different interests, goals, and power**. Decisions are not purely rational—they are the result of **negotiation, bargaining, and political influence**.

> **In simple words:** "Decisions are not just about logic. They are about who has power and how people negotiate."

### Key Assumptions

| Assumption | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Multiple stakeholders** | Different people have different goals and interests. |
| **Conflicting goals** | What is best for marketing may not be best for finance. |
| **Information is power** | Those with information use it to influence decisions. |
| **Coalitions** | Groups form alliances to push their preferred solution. |
| **Bounded rationality** | Also recognizes human limitations. |
| **Satisfactory solution** | The outcome is one that enough people agree on, not necessarily optimal. |

### Key Concepts of the Modern Approach

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Coalition** | Temporary alliance of individuals/groups to support a decision | Marketing and Sales join forces to argue for a larger budget. |
| **Bargaining** | Negotiation to reach an acceptable outcome | "If you support my project, I will support yours next quarter." |
| **Power** | Ability to influence decisions | The Finance manager has power because they control the budget. |
| **Politics** | Activities to gain or use power | Lobbying, forming alliances, controlling information. |

### The Modern Decision Making Process

```
Step 1: Problem is identified (different people may define it differently)
   ↓
Step 2: Different stakeholders have different goals
   ↓
Step 3: Stakeholders form coalitions to support their preferred solution
   ↓
Step 4: Bargaining and negotiation occur
   ↓
Step 5: A solution is selected that is ACCEPTABLE to the powerful coalitions
   ↓
Step 6: Implementation may be influenced by politics
   ↓
Step 7: Outcome is evaluated differently by different groups
```

### Example of Modern Approach

**Situation:** A company must decide where to cut the budget by Rs. 1 crore.

| Stakeholder | Desired Action | Power Level |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Marketing Manager | Cut production budget (not marketing) | Medium |
| Production Manager | Cut marketing budget (not production) | Medium |
| Finance Manager | Cut the department with lowest ROI | High |
| HR Manager | Cut training budget (not salaries) | Low |
| CEO | Wants to keep all departments happy | Highest |

**Political Process:**
- Marketing and Production form a coalition to protect their budgets.
- Finance Manager has data showing which department has lowest ROI.
- CEO mediates and makes final decision after negotiations.

**Final Decision:** Cut training budget and delay a new IT project (acceptable to the most powerful coalitions).

### When is Modern Approach Used?

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| Large organizations with many departments | Corporate budget allocation |
| Decisions with conflicting goals | Resource allocation among divisions |
| Strategic decisions | Mergers, acquisitions, major changes |
| When stakeholders have different interests | New policies, reorganizations |

### Advantages and Limitations

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Recognizes reality of organizational politics | Can be time-consuming (negotiations take time) |
| Accounts for conflicting goals | May produce suboptimal solutions |
| Builds consensus and buy-in | Power may override logic ("might makes right") |
| Considers multiple perspectives | Can lead to conflict and resentment |
| Practical for large organizations | Difficult to study scientifically |

## Which Approach is Best?

| Approach | Best for... |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Classical** | Major strategic decisions with good data, high stakes, and time for analysis. |
| **Administrative** | Most day-to-day, routine, operational decisions. |
| **Modern** | Decisions in large organizations with multiple stakeholders, conflicting goals, and political dynamics. |

> **Key insight:** Effective managers do not use only one approach. They **adapt** based on the situation.

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the different approaches to decision making: Classical, Administrative, and Modern.

**A:**

**Definition of Decision Making:** Decision making is the process of identifying problems, generating alternatives, and choosing a course of action.

**Three Approaches to Decision Making:**

**1. Classical Approach (Rational/Economic Model):**
This approach assumes that managers are perfectly rational, have complete information, and will choose the optimal solution that maximizes organizational goals. The decision maker follows a systematic process: identify the problem, generate all alternatives, evaluate each, and select the best. *Example:* A company chooses a factory location by analyzing all possible cities and selecting the one with the lowest cost. *Limitation:* Assumes perfect information, which is unrealistic.

**2. Administrative Approach (Bounded Rationality - Herbert Simon):**
This realistic approach recognizes that managers face limitations—limited time, limited information, and limited cognitive ability. Therefore, they do not maximize; they "satisfice" (choose the first solution that is "good enough"). *Example:* A manager hires the first job candidate who meets minimum qualifications instead of interviewing all candidates. *Contribution:* Recognizes how managers actually make decisions.

**3. Modern Approach (Behavioral/Political Model):**
This approach recognizes that organizations have multiple stakeholders with different goals, interests, and power. Decisions result from negotiation, bargaining, and coalition-building. Information is used as a source of power. *Example:* A company's budget is allocated not by pure logic but through negotiations between marketing, production, and finance departments. *Contribution:* Recognizes the political reality of large organizations.

**Conclusion:** No single approach is always best. Classical is ideal for major strategic decisions with good data. Administrative is practical for routine decisions. Modern is necessary when multiple stakeholders with conflicting goals are involved.
