## Part 1: Definition of Decision Making

### What is Decision Making?

**Decision making** is the process of **identifying a problem**, **generating alternative solutions**, and **choosing one course of action** from among those alternatives. It is the essence of management.

> **In simple words:** Decision making = choosing the best option when you have choices.

### Nature / Characteristics of Decision Making

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Goal-oriented** | Every decision is made to achieve some objective. |
| **Process** | It is a series of steps, not a single event. |
| **Alternative-based** | No choice = no decision (if only one option exists, no decision is needed). |
| **Intellectual** | Requires reasoning, analysis, and judgment. |
| **Pervasive** | Every manager at every level makes decisions. |
| **Continuous** | Managers make decisions constantly. |
| **Commitment of resources** | Decisions allocate time, money, people, and equipment. |

## Part 2: [[Approaches to Decision Making]]

There are **different approaches** (or models) that explain how managers actually make decisions or should make decisions. They range from **ideal/rational** to **realistic** to **intuitive**.

Here are the **four major approaches**:

| S.No. | Approach | Key Idea | Type |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1 | **Rational/Economic Approach** | Managers make logical, optimal decisions. | Prescriptive (how they *should* decide) |
| 2 | **Bounded Rationality Approach** | Managers are limited, so they "satisfice." | Descriptive (how they *actually* decide) |
| 3 | **Intuitive Approach** | Decisions based on gut feeling and experience. | Descriptive |
| 4 | **Creative Approach** | Decisions based on novel, innovative ideas. | Prescriptive |

Let me explain each approach in detail.

---

## Approach 1: Rational / Economic / Classical Approach

### What is the Rational Approach?

This approach assumes that managers are **perfectly rational**, have **complete information**, and will always choose the **optimal (best possible)** decision to maximize organizational goals.

> **In simple words:** The rational approach says: "I will gather all information, list all options, and pick the absolute best one."

### Assumptions of the Rational Approach

| Assumption | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Complete information** | The manager knows all possible alternatives and all possible outcomes. |
| **No time or cost constraints** | Unlimited time and resources to analyze. |
| **Rationality** | The manager is logical, objective, and consistent. |
| **Goal clarity** | The objective is clear and agreed upon. |
| **Maximization** | The manager chooses the option that gives the highest benefit. |

### Steps in the Rational Decision Making Process

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           RATIONAL DECISION MAKING PROCESS               │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                          │
│  Step 1: Identify the problem                           │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 2: Define the decision criteria                   │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 3: Assign weights to criteria                     │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 4: Generate all possible alternatives             │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 5: Evaluate each alternative                      │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 6: Select the optimal alternative                 │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 7: Implement the decision                         │
│           ↓                                              │
│  Step 8: Evaluate the outcome                           │
│                                                          │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Example of Rational Approach

**Situation:** A company wants to choose a new location for a warehouse.

| Step | Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1. Problem | Current warehouse is too small. |
| 2. Criteria | Cost, distance to customers, labor availability, tax rates. |
| 3. Weights | Cost (40%), Distance (30%), Labor (20%), Tax (10%). |
| 4. Alternatives | City A, City B, City C. |
| 5. Evaluate | Score each city on each criterion. |
| 6. Select | City B has highest total score. |
| 7. Implement | Build warehouse in City B. |
| 8. Evaluate | After 1 year, check if costs and delivery times improved. |

### Advantages and Limitations of Rational Approach

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Logical and systematic | Assumes perfect information (unrealistic) |
| Produces optimal decisions | Too time-consuming for routine decisions |
| Objective and unbiased | Ignores human limitations |
| Easy to justify and defend | Real world is uncertain and complex |

> **Realistic view:** The rational approach is an **ideal** that managers strive for, but rarely achieve perfectly.

## Approach 2: Bounded Rationality Approach (Herbert Simon)

### What is Bounded Rationality?

Herbert Simon (Nobel Prize winner) argued that managers are **not perfectly rational** because they face **limitations** (bounds). Instead of finding the **optimal** solution, they **satisfice**—they choose a solution that is **"good enough."**

> **In simple words:** "I cannot check every option. I will pick the first option that meets my minimum requirements."

### Bounds (Limitations) on Rationality

| Bound | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Limited information** | Managers never have complete information. |
| **Limited time** | Decisions must be made quickly. |
| **Limited cognitive ability** | Human brain cannot process unlimited data. |
| **Limited resources** | No budget for endless analysis. |
| **Uncertainty** | Future outcomes are unknown. |

### Key Concept: Satisficing (vs. Maximizing)

| Term | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Maximizing** | Finding the absolute best option (Rational approach) | Checking every house for sale in the city to find the perfect one |
| **Satisficing** | Finding the first option that is "good enough" (Bounded rationality) | Looking at 5 houses and picking the first one that meets your basic needs |

### Example of Bounded Rationality

**Situation:** A manager needs to hire a new assistant.

- **Rational approach (ideal):** Review 500 resumes, interview 50 candidates, test 20, select the absolute best.
- **Bounded rationality (realistic):** Review resumes until you find 10 good candidates, interview them, hire the first one who meets your minimum requirements (education, experience, attitude).

### Advantages and Limitations of Bounded Rationality

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Realistic and practical | May miss the truly best solution |
| Saves time and effort | "Good enough" may not be very good |
| Works well in routine decisions | Can lead to settling for mediocrity |
| Recognizes human limitations | Depends on quality of minimum standards |

> **Key takeaway:** Most real-world decisions use bounded rationality, not pure rationality.

## Approach 3: Intuitive Approach

### What is Intuitive Decision Making?

**Intuitive decision making** is making decisions based on **gut feeling, instinct, and experience** rather than conscious analysis. It is a subconscious process that draws on years of accumulated expertise.

> **In simple words:** "I don't know why, but this feels right."

### When is Intuition Used?

| Situation | Reason |
| :--- | :--- |
| Time is very short | No time for analysis (emergency) |
| Information is incomplete | Data does not exist |
| High uncertainty | Analysis cannot predict outcomes |
| Highly experienced manager | Past patterns are recognized subconsciously |
| Routine decisions | Analysis is unnecessary |

### Types of Intuitive Decision Making

| Type | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Experience-based** | "I've seen this situation before." |
| **Values-based** | "This feels right morally." |
| **Subconscious** | "I just know, but can't explain why." |
| **Affective** | "I like this option emotionally." |

### Example of Intuitive Approach

**Situation:** A fire chief arrives at a burning building.

- **Analytical approach:** Calculate wind speed, building structure, water pressure, etc. (Too slow!)
- **Intuitive approach:** Based on 20 years of experience, the chief immediately knows: "Get everyone out now—the roof is about to collapse."

### Advantages and Limitations of Intuitive Approach

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Very fast | Can be wrong (gut feelings are not always right) |
| Works under uncertainty | Difficult to justify or explain to others |
| Uses deep experience | Biased by emotions and past traumas |
| No data required | Not reliable for novices or complex new problems |

> **Key takeaway:** Intuition is not magic. It is **pattern recognition** based on deep experience. Novices should not rely on intuition.

## Approach 4: Creative Approach

### What is Creative Decision Making?

**Creative decision making** involves generating **novel, innovative, and original** solutions to problems. It goes beyond existing alternatives and creates something new.

> **In simple words:** "Don't choose from existing options. Invent a new option that no one thought of before."

### The Creative Process (Four Stages)

| Stage | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1. Preparation** | Gather information, define the problem clearly. |
| **2. Incubation** | Step away from the problem. Let subconscious work. |
| **3. Illumination** | The "aha!" moment. The creative insight appears. |
| **4. Verification** | Test and refine the creative idea. |

### Techniques for Creative Decision Making

| Technique | How it works | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Brainstorming** | Group generates many ideas without criticism | "List 50 uses for a brick" |
| **Lateral Thinking** | Approach problem from unusual angles | "How would a child solve this?" |
| **Mind Mapping** | Visual diagram connecting ideas | Central problem with branching solutions |
| **Nominal Group Technique** | Silent idea generation, then voting | Each person writes ideas, then shares |
| **Delphi Technique** | Experts give anonymous opinions, iteratively refined | Multiple rounds of expert surveys |

### Example of Creative Approach

**Situation:** A hotel has a problem: guests complain about slow elevator.

- **Conventional solutions:** Upgrade motor (expensive), add more elevators (very expensive).
- **Creative solution:** Install mirrors next to elevator doors. Guests get distracted looking at themselves and stop complaining about waiting time. (Low cost, innovative.)

### Advantages and Limitations of Creative Approach

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Produces breakthrough solutions | Time-consuming |
| Competitive advantage | Requires special skills and culture |
| Solves problems others cannot | Creative ideas may be risky |
| Engages and motivates employees | May be rejected as "too different" |

## The Reality: Managers Use Multiple Approaches

In practice, managers do not use just one approach. They **combine** them based on the situation.

| Factor | Choose approach |
| :--- | :--- |
| **High importance, high time** | Rational approach |
| **Routine, low importance** | Bounded rationality / satisficing |
| **Emergency, no time** | Intuitive approach |
| **Stuck with old solutions** | Creative approach |

### Example: A CEO's Day

| Decision | Approach Used |
| :--- | :--- |
| Annual budget allocation (3-month process) | Rational |
| Which supplier to order from (routine) | Bounded rationality (satisficing) |
| Responding to a factory fire (urgent) | Intuitive |
| How to enter a new market (stuck) | Creative (brainstorming session) |

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Define decision making and explain the different approaches to decision making.

**A:**

**Definition:** Decision making is the process of identifying a problem, generating alternative solutions, and selecting the best course of action from among those alternatives. It is the heart of management.

**Approaches to Decision Making:**

**1. Rational/Economic Approach:**
This approach assumes that managers have complete information, unlimited time, and perfect rationality. They will identify all alternatives, evaluate them, and choose the optimal (best possible) solution. For example, choosing a factory location after analyzing all possible cities. *Limitation:* Unrealistic assumptions.

**2. Bounded Rationality Approach (Herbert Simon):**
This realistic approach recognizes that managers face limitations—limited time, limited information, limited cognitive ability. Therefore, they do not maximize; they "satisfice" (choose the first solution that is "good enough"). For example, hiring the first candidate who meets minimum qualifications instead of interviewing everyone.

**3. Intuitive Approach:**
This approach relies on gut feeling, instinct, and experience rather than conscious analysis. It is used in emergencies or when data is unavailable. For example, a fire chief deciding to evacuate a building based on 20 years of experience.

**4. Creative Approach:**
This approach generates novel, innovative solutions that did not exist before. It uses techniques like brainstorming and lateral thinking. For example, a hotel installing mirrors to distract guests from slow elevators instead of upgrading the elevators.

**Conclusion:** No single approach is best. Effective managers use a combination of these approaches depending on the situation—rational for major decisions, bounded rationality for routine decisions, intuition for emergencies, and creativity when stuck.
