Great. You've covered **what decision making is** and the **approaches** to it. Now, let's classify the **types of decisions** managers make.

This is a very common exam topic because different types of decisions require different methods, time, and involvement from managers.

Here is a clear, exam-focused explanation of the **Types of Decisions**.

---

## Overview: How to Classify Decisions

Managers make many different types of decisions. They can be classified based on **four main criteria**:

| Basis of Classification                 | Types of Decisions                     |
| :-------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------- |
| **1. Frequency / Repetitiveness**       | Programmed vs. Non-programmed          |
| **2. Level of Management**              | Strategic vs. Tactical vs. Operational |
| **3. Certainty / Knowledge of Outcome** | Certainty vs. Risk vs. Uncertainty     |
| **4. Nature / Impact**                  | Routine vs. Basic / Major              |

> **Most important for exams:** The first two classifications (Programmed vs. Non-programmed and Strategic vs. Tactical vs. Operational) are the most frequently asked.

Let me explain each classification in detail.

---

## Type 1: Based on Frequency / Repetitiveness (Most Important)

This is the **most common** way to classify decisions in management textbooks.

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Programmed Decisions** | Routine, repetitive decisions with established rules or procedures. | Reordering office supplies when stock is low. |
| **Non-programmed Decisions** | Unique, one-time, novel decisions with no ready-made solution. | Launching a new product or entering a new market. |

---

### A. Programmed Decisions (Routine)

**Definition:** Decisions that are **repetitive** and **well-structured**. There are clear rules, policies, or procedures to follow. Managers do not need to create a new solution each time.

> **In simple words:** "We've done this before. Follow the rulebook."

**Characteristics:**

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Repetitive** | Happens frequently (daily, weekly, monthly). |
| **Structured** | Problem is clear and well-defined. |
| **Rules exist** | Policies, procedures, or formulas are available. |
| **Lower management** | Usually made by lower-level managers or even computers. |
| **Low risk** | Mistakes have small consequences. |
| **Quick** | Can be made quickly. |

**Examples of Programmed Decisions:**

| Situation | Programmed Decision Rule |
| :--- | :--- |
| Office printer runs out of paper | "Reorder when stock falls below 2 reams." |
| Customer wants a refund | "Policy: 30-day return with receipt." |
| Employee is late to work | "Rule: 3 late arrivals = written warning." |
| Inventory reaches reorder point | "Automatic purchase order generated." |
| Cashier needs manager approval | "Policy: All refunds over Rs. 5,000 need manager." |

**Advantages of Programmed Decisions:**

| Advantage | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Saves time** | No need to analyze each time. |
| **Consistent** | Same situation gets same response. |
| **Reduces errors** | Following rules prevents mistakes. |
| **Delegable** | Can be handled by junior staff or computers. |
| **Fair** | Everyone treated equally. |

---
### B. Non-programmed Decisions (Non-routine)

**Definition:** Decisions that are **novel**, **unstructured**, and **unique**. There are no existing rules or procedures. Managers must use creativity, judgment, and analysis.

> **In simple words:** "We've never faced this before. We need to figure it out from scratch."

**Characteristics:**

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unique** | Happens rarely or only once. |
| **Unstructured** | Problem is vague and complex. |
| **No existing rules** | Policies and procedures do not exist. |
| **Top management** | Usually made by senior managers. |
| **High risk** | Mistakes have major consequences. |
| **Time-consuming** | Requires analysis and deliberation. |

**Examples of Non-programmed Decisions:**

| Situation | Type of Decision |
| :--- | :--- |
| Should we acquire a competitor? | Strategic acquisition |
| Should we launch a completely new product? | New product development |
| Should we enter a foreign market? | International expansion |
| How to respond to a new competitor? | Competitive response |
| How to handle a major crisis (scandal, disaster)? | Crisis management |

**Advantages and Challenges of Non-programmed Decisions:**

| Advantages | Challenges |
| :--- | :--- |
| Allows creativity and innovation | Time-consuming and expensive |
| Can create competitive advantage | High risk of failure |
| Tailored to specific situation | Requires experienced managers |
| No rigid rules to follow | Hard to justify after the fact |

---

### Comparison Table: Programmed vs. Non-programmed Decisions (Exam-Ready)

| Basis | Programmed Decisions | Non-programmed Decisions |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nature** | Routine, repetitive | Unique, novel |
| **Problem** | Well-structured, clear | Unstructured, vague |
| **Rules** | Existing policies/procedures | No existing rules |
| **Who makes it** | Lower management | Top management |
| **Frequency** | Frequent (daily, weekly) | Rare (once or few times) |
| **Time required** | Very little | Considerable |
| **Risk** | Low | High |
| **Basis** | Habit, rules, formulas | Judgment, creativity, analysis |
| **Example** | Reorder office supplies | Acquire a competitor |
| **Can it be automated?** | Yes (computers can do it) | No (needs human judgment) |

---

## Type 2: Based on Level of Management

Different management levels make different **types of decisions**.

| Level                               | Type of Decision      | Time Horizon               | Example                                                   |
| :---------------------------------- | :-------------------- | :------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Top Management** (CEO, Board)     | Strategic Decisions   | Long-term (3-10+ years)    | "Should we diversify into a new industry?"                |
| **Middle Management** (Dept. Heads) | Tactical Decisions    | Medium-term (1-3 years)    | "How will our marketing department support the strategy?" |
| **Lower Management** (Supervisors)  | Operational Decisions | Short-term (daily, weekly) | "Who will work the night shift tomorrow?"                 |

---

### A. Strategic Decisions (Top Management)

**Definition:** Decisions that affect the **entire organization** for a **long period of time**. They determine the direction and scope of the organization.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Long-term** | 3-10+ years |
| **High risk** | Mistakes can ruin the organization |
| **Large resource commitment** | Involves major investment |
| **Organization-wide** | Affects all departments |
| **Non-programmed** | Unique, no ready-made solutions |

**Examples:**
- Entering a new market or country
- Merging with or acquiring another company
- Launching a completely new product line
- Changing the organization's mission or vision

---

### B. Tactical Decisions (Middle Management)

**Definition:** Decisions that translate **strategic decisions** into **specific actions** for a particular department or function.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Medium-term** | 1-3 years |
| **Medium risk** | Mistakes affect one department |
| **Department focus** | Affects only one function |
| **Connects strategy to action** | Links top and lower levels |

**Examples:**
- Setting a departmental budget
- Deciding on a marketing campaign for the next quarter
- Choosing a new supplier for raw materials
- Deciding on employee training programs

---

### C. Operational Decisions (Lower Management)

**Definition:** Day-to-day decisions about **running the organization** and completing specific tasks.

| Characteristic | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Short-term** | Daily, weekly, monthly |
| **Low risk** | Mistakes are small and correctable |
| **Narrow focus** | Affects a specific task or team |
| **High frequency** | Made many times each day |
| **Programmed** | Often follows rules and procedures |

**Examples:**
- Assigning staff to shifts
- Deciding how much raw material to order today
- Approving overtime for a worker
- Resolving a customer complaint

---

### Comparison Table: Strategic vs. Tactical vs. Operational Decisions

| Basis | Strategic | Tactical | Operational |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Who makes it** | Top management | Middle management | Lower management |
| **Time horizon** | Long-term (3-10+ years) | Medium-term (1-3 years) | Short-term (daily/weekly) |
| **Risk** | High | Medium | Low |
| **Scope** | Entire organization | Department | Team or individual |
| **Frequency** | Rare | Moderate | Very frequent |
| **Nature** | Non-programmed | Mixed | Programmed |
| **Example** | Enter a new country | Plan a marketing campaign | Schedule tomorrow's shifts |

---

## Type 3: Based on Certainty (Knowledge of Outcome)

Decisions can be classified by **how much the manager knows** about the outcome of each alternative.

| Type | What it means | Decision Rule | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Certainty** | Manager knows exactly what will happen with each alternative. | Choose the one with best outcome (maximize). | Buying raw materials at a fixed price. |
| **Risk** | Manager knows the probability of each outcome (e.g., 70% chance of success). | Choose based on expected value. | Investing in a new machine with known success probability. |
| **Uncertainty** | Manager has no information about probabilities or outcomes. | Use judgment, intuition, or creativity. | Entering a completely new foreign market. |

---

### A. Decisions under Certainty

**Definition:** The manager knows **exactly** what the outcome of each alternative will be. All information is available.

**Example:**
- A company can buy steel at Rs. 50,000 per ton from Supplier A or Rs. 52,000 from Supplier B.
- **Decision:** Choose Supplier A (lower cost). Outcome is certain.

---

### B. Decisions under Risk

**Definition:** The manager does not know the exact outcome, but **knows the probability** (likelihood) of each possible outcome.

**Example:**
- A company launches a new product.
- 70% probability of success (profit Rs. 10 crore)
- 30% probability of failure (loss Rs. 5 crore)
- **Expected value** = (0.7 × 10) + (0.3 × -5) = 7 - 1.5 = Rs. 5.5 crore expected profit.

---

### C. Decisions under Uncertainty

**Definition:** The manager knows neither the outcomes nor the probabilities. This is the most difficult situation.

**Example:**
- A company considers entering a new country's market.
- No historical data exists.
- No one knows the probability of success or failure.
- **Decision must be based on:** Intuition, judgment, research, or scenario planning.

---

## Type 4: Based on Nature / Impact

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Routine Decisions** | Everyday, repetitive decisions. | Which pen to buy for the office. |
| **Basic / Major Decisions** | Important, strategic decisions with long-term impact. | Whether to build a new factory. |

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the different types of decisions managers make, with examples.

**A:**

**Definition:** Decision making is the process of choosing among alternatives. Managers make many different types of decisions, which can be classified in several ways.

**Types of Decisions:**

**1. Based on Frequency: Programmed vs. Non-programmed Decisions**

| Type | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Programmed** | Routine, repetitive decisions with established rules | Reordering office supplies when stock is low |
| **Non-programmed** | Unique, novel decisions with no ready-made solution | Deciding whether to acquire a competitor |

**2. Based on Management Level: Strategic vs. Tactical vs. Operational**

| Type | Who | Time | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Strategic** | Top management | Long-term | Entering a new country |
| **Tactical** | Middle management | Medium-term | Setting a departmental budget |
| **Operational** | Lower management | Short-term | Assigning daily work shifts |

**3. Based on Certainty: Certainty vs. Risk vs. Uncertainty**

| Type | Knowledge | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Certainty** | Exact outcome known | Buying from the cheapest supplier |
| **Risk** | Probabilities known | Launching a product with 70% success chance |
| **Uncertainty** | No information available | Entering a completely new market |

**Conclusion:** Different types of decisions require different approaches. Programmed, operational decisions can be handled by rules and lower managers. Non-programmed, strategic decisions require top management judgment, creativity, and analysis.
