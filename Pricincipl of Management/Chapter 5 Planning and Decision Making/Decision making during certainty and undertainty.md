## Part 1: Decision Making under Certainty

### What is Certainty?

**Certainty** exists when the manager knows **exactly** what the outcome of each alternative will be. There is no doubt, no risk, and no ambiguity. The manager has **complete information**.

> **In simple words:** "If I choose option A, I know exactly what will happen."

### Conditions for Certainty

| Condition | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Complete information** | All relevant data is available. |
| **Deterministic outcomes** | Each action leads to a single, known outcome. |
| **No uncertainty** | The future is perfectly predictable. |
| **Clear cause-effect** | The relationship between action and result is known. |

### When Does Certainty Occur?

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Simple, routine decisions** | Buying raw materials at a fixed price. |
| **Short-term decisions** | "If we produce 100 units, we know the cost." |
| **Decisions with fixed contracts** | "Supplier will deliver at Rs. 500 per unit." |
| **Decisions in stable environments** | Established industries with predictable demand. |

### Methods / Tools for Decisions under Certainty

Since outcomes are known, the manager simply needs to **compare** alternatives and choose the best one.

| Method | How it works | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Simple Comparison** | List each alternative and its outcome; pick highest profit or lowest cost. | Choose supplier with lowest price. |
| **Break-Even Analysis** | Find the quantity where costs equal revenue. | How many units to sell to cover costs? |
| **Linear Programming** | Optimize resource allocation with known constraints. | Maximize profit with limited labor and materials. |
| **Marginal Analysis** | Compare additional benefit vs. additional cost. | Should we hire one more worker? |

### Example of Decision under Certainty

**Scenario:** A bakery needs to buy 500 kg of flour. Three suppliers quote:

| Supplier | Price per kg | Total Cost (500 kg) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Supplier A | Rs. 50 | Rs. 25,000 |
| Supplier B | Rs. 52 | Rs. 26,000 |
| Supplier C | Rs. 48 | Rs. 24,000 |

**Decision:** Choose Supplier C (lowest price).

**Outcome is certain:** The bakery will pay exactly Rs. 24,000. No surprises.

### Another Example: Make or Buy Decision (Certainty)

**Scenario:** A company needs 1,000 components.

| Option | Cost Calculation | Total Cost |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Make in-house** | Rs. 200 per unit (materials + labor) | Rs. 200,000 |
| **Buy from supplier** | Rs. 220 per unit | Rs. 220,000 |

**Decision:** Make in-house (cheaper by Rs. 20,000).

**Outcome is certain:** Costs are known exactly.

### Advantages and Limitations of Decisions under Certainty

| Advantages | Limitations |
| :--- | :--- |
| Easy and quick to decide | Rare in real business world |
| No risk of surprises | Most important decisions involve uncertainty |
| Simple tools work well | Assumes perfect knowledge (unrealistic) |
| Easy to justify and explain | Cannot handle unknown factors |

> **Real-world note:** True certainty is **rare** in business. Most decisions involve at least some risk or uncertainty. Certainty is mostly found in routine, operational, or short-term decisions.

## Part 2: Decision Making under Uncertainty

### What is Uncertainty?

**Uncertainty** exists when the manager **does not know** the outcomes of alternatives or the probabilities of those outcomes. Information is incomplete, and the future is unpredictable.

> **In simple words:** "I have no idea what will happen if I choose option A, B, or C."

### Conditions for Uncertainty

| Condition | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Incomplete information** | Relevant data is missing or unavailable. |
| **Unknown outcomes** | Cannot predict what will happen. |
| **Unknown probabilities** | Do not know the likelihood of events. |
| **Novel situation** | No past experience to guide decisions. |
| **Dynamic environment** | Conditions are changing rapidly. |

### When Does Uncertainty Occur?

| Situation | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **New markets** | Entering a country where your product has never been sold. |
| **New technology** | Deciding whether to invest in an unproven technology. |
| **Major crises** | Responding to a pandemic, natural disaster, or war. |
| **Innovation** | Launching a completely new type of product. |
| **Political instability** | Investing in a country with an unstable government. |

### Methods / Criteria for Decisions under Uncertainty

Since outcomes and probabilities are unknown, managers use **decision criteria** (rules or strategies) to choose. The choice depends on the manager's **attitude toward risk** (optimistic, pessimistic, or neutral).

Here are the **five main criteria** for decision making under uncertainty:

| Criterion | Attitude | Decision Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Maximax (Optimistic)** | Risk-seeking / Optimist | Choose the alternative with the best possible (maximum) payoff. |
| **2. Maximin (Pessimistic)** | Risk-averse / Pessimist | Choose the alternative that gives the best worst-case payoff. |
| **3. Minimax Regret (Regret)** | Minimize regret | Choose the alternative that minimizes maximum regret (opportunity loss). |
| **4. Laplace (Equal Probability)** | Neutral | Assume all outcomes are equally likely; choose highest average payoff. |
| **5. Hurwicz (Realism)** | Balanced | Weighted average of best and worst outcomes (choose alpha between 0 and 1). |

Let me explain each with a common example.

---

### The Decision Matrix (Example)

**Scenario:** A company can choose one of three investment strategies. The outcome depends on the **future state of the economy** (which is unknown). No probabilities are known.

| Strategy | Economy Grows | Economy Stable | Economy Declines |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Strategy A: Expand aggressively** | Rs. 10 crore | Rs. 4 crore | Rs. -3 crore (loss) |
| **Strategy B: Expand cautiously** | Rs. 6 crore | Rs. 5 crore | Rs. 1 crore |
| **Strategy C: No expansion** | Rs. 2 crore | Rs. 2 crore | Rs. 2 crore |

Now let me apply each decision criterion.

---

### Criterion 1: Maximax (Optimistic)

**Rule:** Choose the alternative with the **maximum possible payoff** (best of the best). This is for **risk-seeking** or **optimistic** managers.

| Strategy | Best Possible Outcome |
| :--- | :--- |
| A | Rs. 10 crore |
| B | Rs. 6 crore |
| C | Rs. 2 crore |

**Decision:** Choose **Strategy A** (best possible = Rs. 10 crore if economy grows).

> **Motto:** "Go big or go home. Hope for the best."

---

### Criterion 2: Maximin (Pessimistic)

**Rule:** Choose the alternative with the **maximum of the minimum payoffs** (best of the worst). This is for **risk-averse** or **pessimistic** managers.

| Strategy | Worst Possible Outcome |
| :--- | :--- |
| A | Rs. -3 crore (loss) |
| B | Rs. 1 crore |
| C | Rs. 2 crore |

**Decision:** Choose **Strategy C** (worst case = Rs. 2 crore, which is better than losing money).

> **Motto:** "Hope for the best, but prepare for the worst."

---

### Criterion 3: Minimax Regret (Regret / Opportunity Loss)

**Rule:** Choose the alternative that **minimizes the maximum regret**. Regret = what you lose by not choosing the best alternative for each state of nature.

> **In simple words:** "Which decision will I regret the least if things go wrong?"

**Step 1: Create a Regret Matrix**

For each state of nature, find the **best payoff**. Regret = (Best payoff in that state) − (Actual payoff for your alternative).

| State | Best Payoff | Regret for A | Regret for B | Regret for C |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Economy Grows | Rs. 10 (A) | 10−10 = 0 | 10−6 = 4 | 10−2 = 8 |
| Economy Stable | Rs. 5 (B) | 5−4 = 1 | 5−5 = 0 | 5−2 = 3 |
| Economy Declines | Rs. 2 (C) | 2−(−3) = 5 | 2−1 = 1 | 2−2 = 0 |

**Regret Matrix:**

| Strategy | Regret (Grows) | Regret (Stable) | Regret (Declines) | Maximum Regret |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| A | 0 | 1 | 5 | **5** |
| B | 4 | 0 | 1 | **4** |
| C | 8 | 3 | 0 | **8** |

**Step 2: Choose the strategy with the smallest maximum regret**

- A = max regret 5
- B = max regret 4 ← **Lowest**
- C = max regret 8

**Decision:** Choose **Strategy B** (minimizes maximum regret).

> **Motto:** "I want to avoid kicking myself later."

---

### Criterion 4: Laplace (Equal Probability / Rationality)

**Rule:** Assume all states of nature are **equally likely**. Calculate the average payoff for each strategy. Choose the highest average.

| Strategy | Average Payoff |
| :--- | :--- |
| A | (10 + 4 + (-3)) ÷ 3 = 11 ÷ 3 = **Rs. 3.67 crore** |
| B | (6 + 5 + 1) ÷ 3 = 12 ÷ 3 = **Rs. 4.00 crore** |
| C | (2 + 2 + 2) ÷ 3 = 6 ÷ 3 = **Rs. 2.00 crore** |

**Decision:** Choose **Strategy B** (highest average = Rs. 4 crore).

> **Motto:** "Since I don't know, I'll assume everything is equally likely."

---

### Criterion 5: Hurwicz (Realism / Alpha)

**Rule:** This criterion allows the manager to choose a **coefficient of optimism** (α) between 0 and 1.

- α = coefficient of optimism (0 to 1)
- (1−α) = coefficient of pessimism

**Formula:** Weighted value = α × (Best outcome) + (1−α) × (Worst outcome)

**Example:** Let α = 0.6 (60% optimistic, 40% pessimistic)

| Strategy | Best | Worst | Weighted Value (α=0.6) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| A | 10 | -3 | 0.6×10 + 0.4×(-3) = 6 − 1.2 = **4.8** |
| B | 6 | 1 | 0.6×6 + 0.4×1 = 3.6 + 0.4 = **4.0** |
| C | 2 | 2 | 0.6×2 + 0.4×2 = 1.2 + 0.8 = **2.0** |

**Decision:** Choose **Strategy A** (highest weighted value = 4.8).

> **Note:** If α = 1 (completely optimistic) → chooses A. If α = 0 (completely pessimistic) → chooses C.

---

### Summary Table: Decisions under Uncertainty (Example Results)

| Criterion | Attitude | Decision |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Maximax** | Optimistic / Risk-seeking | Strategy A |
| **Maximin** | Pessimistic / Risk-averse | Strategy C |
| **Minimax Regret** | Minimize regret | Strategy B |
| **Laplace** | Neutral (equal probability) | Strategy B |
| **Hurwicz (α=0.6)** | Balanced realism | Strategy A (with 60% optimism) |

> **Key insight:** Different criteria can lead to **different decisions**. There is no single "right" answer under uncertainty. The manager's risk attitude determines the choice.

## Risk vs. Uncertainty (Important Distinction)

Many students confuse risk and uncertainty. Here is the clear difference:

| Condition | What you know | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Certainty** | Exact outcome known | "If I buy this bond, I will get 10% return guaranteed." |
| **Risk** | Outcomes and probabilities known | "70% chance of success, 30% chance of failure." |
| **Uncertainty** | Neither outcomes nor probabilities known | "I have no idea what will happen." |

> **Memory trick:**
> - **Certainty** = 100% sure
> - **Risk** = Roll the dice (you know the odds)
> - **Uncertainty** = Blindfolded (no idea)

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** What is the difference between decision making under certainty and decision making under uncertainty? Explain with examples and the methods used for each.

**A:**

**Decision Making under Certainty:**

Certainty exists when the manager knows exactly what the outcome of each alternative will be. There is complete information and no risk.

- **Example:** A company chooses between three suppliers with known, fixed prices. The decision is simple: choose the lowest price.
- **Methods used:** Simple comparison, break-even analysis, linear programming.
- **Decision rule:** Choose the alternative with the highest profit or lowest cost.

**Decision Making under Uncertainty:**

Uncertainty exists when the manager does not know the outcomes of alternatives or the probabilities of those outcomes. Information is incomplete.

- **Example:** A company decides whether to enter a new foreign market. No one knows what will happen.
- **Methods used:** Maximax (optimistic), Maximin (pessimistic), Minimax Regret, Laplace (equal probability), Hurwicz (realism).
- **Decision rule:** Depends on the manager's attitude toward risk (optimistic, pessimistic, or neutral).

**Key Differences:**

| Basis | Certainty | Uncertainty |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Information | Complete | Incomplete |
| Outcomes | Known | Unknown |
| Risk | None | High |
| Difficulty | Easy | Difficult |
| Example | Buying from cheapest supplier | Entering a new market |

**Conclusion:** Most routine, operational decisions are made under certainty or risk. Most strategic, innovative decisions are made under uncertainty. Managers must recognize which condition they face and choose the appropriate method.
