Perfect follow-up. You already understand the **concept** (what and why) and **approaches** (how much). Now, **areas of social responsibility** tells you *towards whom* or *in which fields* a business must be responsible.

Here is a clear, exam-focused explanation of the **key areas of social responsibility**.

---

## The Big Picture

A business operates within society and affects many different groups. Therefore, its social responsibility is **multi-dimensional**. These dimensions are called **areas of social responsibility**.

> **Simple definition:** The specific groups (stakeholders) or issues (domains) toward which a business has an ethical and legal obligation.

The most widely accepted framework divides social responsibility into **five major areas**:

---

## The Five Key Areas of Social Responsibility

| Area | Responsible Towards | Key Obligations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1. Economic Responsibility** | Owners, shareholders, employees | Earn profit, provide returns, create jobs |
| **2. Legal Responsibility** | Government, society | Obey all laws and regulations |
| **3. Ethical Responsibility** | Society, stakeholders | Do what is right, fair, and just (beyond law) |
| **4. Discretionary (Philanthropic) Responsibility** | Community, society | Voluntary charity, social welfare, community service |
| **5. Environmental Responsibility** | Nature, future generations | Protect natural resources, reduce pollution |

> **Note:** Some textbooks combine Ethical + Discretionary, or list Environmental separately (as I have done, because it's very important today). Others present these as the **CSR pyramid** (Carroll's Pyramid).

---

## Detailed Explanation of Each Area

### 1. Economic Responsibility (The Foundation)

This is the most basic area. A business must **be profitable** to survive and serve others.

| Obligation | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Earn fair profit | Cover costs and provide return to owners/shareholders. |
| Create jobs | Provide employment and livelihoods. |
| Produce useful goods/services | Satisfy customer needs. |
| Pay fair wages | Reward employees fairly for their work. |
| Innovate | Develop new products and efficient processes. |

> **Without economic responsibility, all other responsibilities are impossible.** A loss-making company cannot donate to charity or install pollution controls.

### 2. Legal Responsibility (Obedience to Law)

A business must operate **within the framework of the law**. This is the minimum expected by society.

| Obligation | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Follow labor laws | Minimum wage, safety, working hours, no child labor. |
| Follow tax laws | Pay all taxes honestly and on time. |
| Follow environmental laws | Pollution limits, waste disposal rules. |
| Follow consumer protection laws | Accurate labeling, no false advertising, product safety. |
| Follow competition laws | No price-fixing, no unfair monopolies. |

> **Example:** A company that pays taxes and follows factory safety laws is meeting legal responsibility.

### 3. Ethical Responsibility (Doing What is Right — Beyond Law)

This area goes beyond mere legal compliance. Society expects businesses to act **fairly, honestly, and morally**, even when no law requires it.

| Obligation | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Honest advertising | No misleading claims, even if technically legal. |
| Fair treatment of employees | No discrimination by race, gender, religion; respect dignity. |
| Fair dealing with suppliers | Pay on time, don't exploit small vendors. |
| Transparency | Disclose genuine information to investors and customers. |
| Avoiding conflict of interest | Managers should not use company position for personal gain. |

> **Example:** A company voluntarily recalls a defective product before any law forces it. That's ethical responsibility.

### 4. Discretionary (Philanthropic) Responsibility (Voluntary Giving)

This area is purely **voluntary**. Society does not demand it, but appreciates it greatly. It includes charitable, educational, and cultural contributions.

| Obligation | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Charitable donations | Give money to hospitals, schools, disaster relief. |
| Community development | Build roads, parks, libraries, drinking water facilities. |
| Support for arts and culture | Sponsor music, dance, theater, museums. |
| Employee volunteer programs | Allow staff paid time off to volunteer for causes. |
| Scholarships and education grants | Help poor or meritorious students. |

> **Example:** Tata Group runs cancer hospitals and IIT Bombay's research center. That's philanthropy.

### 5. Environmental Responsibility (Protecting Nature)

This area has become critical in recent decades. Businesses must ensure their operations do not harm the **natural environment** — for current and future generations.

| Obligation | Explanation |
| :--- | :--- |
| Reduce pollution | Air, water, and soil pollution from factories. |
| Proper waste management | Recycle, treat toxic waste, reduce single-use plastic. |
| Conserve resources | Use energy-efficient machinery, save water, use renewable energy. |
| Sustainable sourcing | Buy raw materials from eco-friendly sources (e.g., sustainable wood). |
| Carbon footprint reduction | Reduce greenhouse gas emissions; plant trees. |

> **Example:** A company installing solar panels and using recycled packaging is meeting environmental responsibility.

## Carroll's Pyramid of CSR (Visual Framework)

Most management textbooks present these areas as a **pyramid**, with Economic at the base (most fundamental) and Philanthropic at the top (least required but most admired).

```
               /\
              /  \
             /    \
            /  Philanthropic  \   ← Desired (Give back)
           /    (Discretionary) \
          /----------------------\
         /       Ethical         \   ← Expected (Do right)
        /         (Moral)         \
       /----------------------------\
      /          Legal               \   ← Required (Obey law)
     /          (Compliance)           \
    /------------------------------------\
   /          Economic (Profit)           \  ← Fundamental (Survive)
  /         (Financial Viability)          \
 /--------------------------------------------\
```

| Level | Area | Society's Expectation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Top | Philanthropic | **Desired** (Good if done, not blamed if not) |
| 2nd | Ethical | **Expected** (Blamed if not done) |
| 3rd | Legal | **Required** (Punished if violated) |
| Base | Economic | **Fundamental** (Must have to exist) |

## Real-World Example: A Single Company (ITC Limited)

ITC (Indian tobacco-to-hotels conglomerate) is often cited for covering all areas:

| Area | ITC's Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Economic** | Generates large profits, employs thousands, pays taxes. |
| **Legal** | Complies with all company, labor, and tax laws. |
| **Ethical** | No false advertising; fair dealing with farmers (ePaper division). |
| **Philanthropic** | Runs e-Choupal (digital village programs) and rural development. |
| **Environmental** | All ITC hotels are LEED certified (green buildings); zero waste to landfill. |

## Exam-Style Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the major areas of social responsibility of a business.

**A:**

Social responsibility of business is not limited to one dimension. The major areas are:

1.  **Economic Responsibility:** The business must earn a profit, provide returns to shareholders, create jobs, and produce useful goods and services. This is the foundation for all other responsibilities.

2.  **Legal Responsibility:** The business must obey all laws — labor laws, tax laws, environmental laws, consumer protection laws. This is the minimum required by society.

3.  **Ethical Responsibility:** Beyond law, the business must act fairly, honestly, and morally — e.g., honest advertising, no discrimination, fair dealing with suppliers.

4.  **Discretionary (Philanthropic) Responsibility:** Voluntary actions to improve community welfare, such as donations to schools, hospitals, disaster relief, and cultural programs.

5.  **Environmental Responsibility:** The business must protect natural resources, reduce pollution, manage waste, and adopt sustainable practices to ensure a healthy planet for future generations.

Together, these five areas ensure that a business is a responsible citizen and contributes to sustainable development.
