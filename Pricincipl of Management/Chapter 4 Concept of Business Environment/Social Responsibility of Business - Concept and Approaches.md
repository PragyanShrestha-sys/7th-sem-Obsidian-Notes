## Part 1: Concept of Social Responsibility of Business

### What is Social Responsibility? (Definition)

**Social Responsibility of Business** (also called Corporate Social Responsibility or CSR) refers to the **obligation of a business to protect and serve the interests of society**, not just its own shareholders or owners.

> **In simple words:** A business must earn profits legally and ethically, but also contribute to the well-being of employees, customers, communities, and the environment.

### The Traditional vs. Modern View

| | **Traditional View** (Milton Friedman) | **Modern View** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Belief** | Business has only one responsibility: to maximize profit for shareholders. | Business has multiple responsibilities: to all stakeholders (owners, employees, customers, society, environment). |
| **Famous quote** | "The business of business is business." | "Business cannot succeed in a failing society." |

### Why is Social Responsibility Important? (Arguments in Favor)

1.  **Long-term survival:** A business that harms society will face boycotts, laws, and bad reputation.
2.  **Public image:** Socially responsible companies attract more customers and better employees.
3.  **Government regulation:** Voluntary responsibility reduces the need for strict laws.
4.  **Stakeholder satisfaction:** Employees, customers, and investors prefer ethical businesses.
5.  **Sustainable development:** Protects resources for future generations.

> **Example:** Tata Group in India is known for CSR (hospitals, schools, water purification). Their reputation builds customer trust.

### Towards Whom is Business Socially Responsible? (Stakeholders)

A business has responsibilities to **different groups**:

| Stakeholder | Responsibility of Business |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Owners/Shareholders** | Provide fair returns, honest financial reporting |
| **Employees** | Fair wages, safe workplace, respect, growth opportunities |
| **Customers** | Quality products, fair prices, honest advertising, after-sales service |
| **Suppliers** | Timely payments, fair dealing |
| **Government** | Pay taxes honestly, obey laws |
| **Community/Society** | No pollution, create jobs, support local causes |
| **Environment** | Reduce waste, use sustainable resources |

## Part 2: Approaches to Social Responsibility

Not all businesses take responsibility equally. There is a **spectrum** of approaches, ranging from avoiding responsibility to proactively leading social change.

These are the **four main approaches** (from lowest to highest responsibility):

| Approach              | Attitude                                        | Behavior                                                      | Example                                                                             |
| :-------------------- | :---------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------ | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Obstructionist** | "We will fight against responsibility."         | Actively hides wrongdoing, violates laws, fights regulation.  | A factory secretly dumping toxic waste at night.                                    |
| **2. Defensive**      | "We will do only what the law forces us to do." | Complies minimally with laws, but no voluntary action.        | A company follows pollution laws only to avoid fines.                               |
| **3. Accommodative**  | "We will do what is asked or expected."         | Meets legal and ethical norms; agrees to reasonable requests. | A firm donates to a local school when the community asks.                           |
| **4. Proactive**      | "We will lead and go beyond expectations."      | Seeks opportunities to help society even when not required.   | Patagonia (clothing brand) donates 1% of sales to environmental causes voluntarily. |

### Detailed Explanation of Each Approach

#### 1. Obstructionist Approach (Lowest Responsibility)

- **Motto:** "Ignore or fight against responsibility."
- **Behavior:** Denies problems, breaks laws, hides evidence, fights regulators.
- **Risk:** High legal penalties, public outrage, loss of license.
- **Example:** Enron (accounting fraud), Volkswagen (diesel emission cheating).

#### 2. Defensive Approach (Social Obligation)

- **Motto:** "Follow the law, nothing more."
- **Behavior:** Meets minimum legal standards. Does only what is required to avoid punishment.
- **Reality check:** Many small businesses operate at this level due to cost constraints.
- **Example:** A factory installs basic pollution control equipment only after being fined once.

#### 3. Accommodative Approach (Social Response)

- **Motto:** "Do the right thing when asked or expected."
- **Behavior:** Meets legal and ethical norms. Willing to negotiate and adapt to stakeholder demands.
- **Characteristics:** Reacts to social pressure rather than anticipating it.
- **Example:** A company starts a recycling program after customers write letters demanding it.

#### 4. Proactive Approach (Social Contribution) (Highest Responsibility) 

- **Motto:** "Lead and go beyond expectations."
- **Behavior:** Actively seeks ways to help society. Invests in CSR even when not required by law or pressure.
- **Characteristics:** Sets industry standards. Views social responsibility as an opportunity, not a cost.
- **Example:** Unilever's Sustainable Living Plan, Tata's community hospitals.

## Which Approach is Best?

In **Principles of Management**, the **Proactive** approach is considered ideal for long-term success. However, in reality:

- Most businesses start with **Defensive** or **Accommodative**.
- Large, successful companies aim for **Proactive**.
- **Obstructionist** often leads to failure, fines, or closure.

> **Exam tip:** When asked for your opinion, say that a business should at least be **Accommodative** and strive toward **Proactive** for sustainable success.

## Sample Exam Question & Answer

**Q:** Explain the concept of social responsibility of business and describe any four approaches to it.

**A:**

**Concept:** Social responsibility means a business has an obligation to act ethically and contribute to the welfare of society, not just maximize profit for owners. It includes responsibilities toward employees, customers, suppliers, government, community, and environment.

**Approaches (four):**

1.  **Obstructionist approach:** The business actively fights against social responsibility, violates laws, and hides wrongdoing. Example: Dumping toxic waste secretly.
2.  **Defensive approach:** The business does only what is legally required, with no voluntary effort. Example: Installing pollution control only to avoid fines.
3.  **Accommodative approach:** The business meets legal and ethical norms and responds when society asks for something. Example: Starting a recycling program after customer requests.
4.  **Proactive approach:** The business goes beyond expectations, voluntarily seeking ways to help society. Example: Donating a percentage of profits to education or environment.

Among these, the proactive approach is most desirable for long-term reputation and sustainability.
