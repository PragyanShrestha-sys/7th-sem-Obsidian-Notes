
### The 7 Defining Characteristics of a Group

| Characteristic                          | Explanation (What it means)                                                                                                                                                                                   | Real-World Example                                                                                                                                                       |
| :-------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Two or More Persons**              | The minimum requirement for a group is a collection of individuals. A single person cannot form a group. The size can be a small dyad (2 people) or a large department.                                       | A team of 5 software developers working on a mobile app.                                                                                                                 |
| **2. Collective Identity (We-Feeling)** | Members must psychologically identify with the group. They use "**We**" instead of "I" or "They." They feel a sense of belonging, loyalty, and emotional attachment to the group.                             | A marketing executive says, *"**We** are the best team in this company,"* rather than *"I work in the marketing department."*                                            |
| **3. Common Purpose / Shared Goal**     | Every group exists for a reason. All members must be aligned and working toward a single, collective objective that they cannot achieve alone. Without a common goal, they are just strangers.                | The group’s shared goal is: *"Launch the new product successfully within 3 months and achieve 10,000 first-day sales."*                                                  |
| **4. Interaction among Members**        | Members must communicate and interact with each other regularly. This interaction can be face-to-face, via email, Zoom calls, or internal messaging apps. Interaction is the fuel that keeps the group alive. | The team holds a daily 15-minute "stand-up" meeting and constantly chats on Slack to share updates and clarify doubts.                                                   |
| **5. Interdependence**                  | The actions of one member directly affect the others. They rely on each other’s work to get the job done. If one person fails, the whole group suffers. They "sink or swim" together.                         | If the UI designer is late with the mockups, the front-end developer cannot start coding, which delays the entire product launch.                                        |
| **6. Group Norms (Rules of Behavior)**  | Every group develops its own unwritten rules of acceptable behavior. These norms dictate how members should dress, speak, work, and treat each other. Formal groups also have written policies.               | Norms like: *"We never throw a teammate under the bus in front of the boss,"* or *"We always help new joiners settle in,"* or *"We do not gossip about absent members."* |
| **7. Authority Structure (Hierarchy)**  | Every group has a formal or informal authority structure. There is always a leader (appointed or emergent) and a clear (or understood) ranking of who holds power, makes decisions, and assigns duties.       | The group has a Project Manager (formal leader) who assigns tasks, and a senior developer (informal leader) whom everyone else goes to for technical advice.             |

---

### ⚠️ The Classic Exam Distinction (Crowd vs. Group)

To absolutely *cement* your understanding, let's compare a group to a mere "crowd." Examiners often slip in a tricky question like: *"Is a crowd a group?"* 

| Feature | A Group | A Crowd (Not a Group!) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Interaction** | Members actively interact and talk to each other. | No meaningful interaction; everyone is isolated. |
| **Goal** | Shares a common, specific objective. | People have individual, temporary goals. |
| **Structure** | Has a leader and defined roles. | No structure, no leader, no organization. |
| **We-Feeling** | High sense of belonging ("We are a team"). | No sense of belonging; people are anonymous. |
| **Example** | 10 surgeons working together on a complex heart transplant. | 500 people waiting for a train at a busy railway station. |

---

### The Golden Exam Conclusion Paragraph:

> *"A true group is far more than just a collection of individuals. It is characterized by **mutual interaction, a shared mission, and a deep sense of interdependence**. Members of a group are bound together by **norms** and recognize a clear **authority structure**. When these characteristics are strong, the group generates a powerful 'we-feeling' that boosts morale and unlocks collective synergy. Managers who understand these characteristics can effectively diagnose team health—if any characteristic is missing (like interdependence or common goals), the team will underperform and require immediate intervention."*

### 🎯 Your Quick Mental Checklist to Spot a Group:

1.  ✅ At least 2 people.
2.  ✅ They talk to each other regularly (Interaction).
3.  ✅ They share a specific goal (Common Purpose).
4.  ✅ They depend on each other to finish work (Interdependence).
5.  ✅ They feel like "We" (Collective Identity).
6.  ✅ They have unwritten rules (Norms).
7.  ✅ They have a leader (Authority).
