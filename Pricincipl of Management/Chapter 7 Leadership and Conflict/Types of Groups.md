Since we just covered **how** groups form (Tuckman’s stages), it’s the perfect time to lock in **what** types of groups exist. 

Examiners love this topic because it tests whether you understand the *social reality* of an organization—not just the official hierarchy, but the invisible human connections that truly make it run.

Here is your complete, crisp, exam-ready breakdown of the **Types of Groups**. 

---

### The Big Picture (The Golden Rule)
All groups in an organization fall under two massive umbrellas: **Formal** (created by the company) and **Informal** (created by employees themselves). 

---

### 1. Formal Groups (The "Official" Structure)

**Definition:** These are created deliberately by management to achieve specific organizational goals. They have a defined structure, assigned roles, and a fixed authority hierarchy. Membership is mandatory, and they operate strictly according to rules and policies.

#### Sub-types of Formal Groups:

| Sub-Type | Meaning | Lifespan | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Command Group** | A permanent group consisting of a manager and all the subordinates who directly report to that manager. This is the basic building block of the organizational chart. | **Permanent** (as long as the department exists). | A Sales Manager and their 10 regional sales executives. The Head of Finance and their 5 accountants. |
| **Task Group / Project Group** | A temporary group formed to work on a specific, one-time project or task. Once the mission is accomplished, the group dissolves. | **Temporary** (dissolves after project completion). | A special committee formed to plan the company’s Annual General Meeting, or a task force to research a new product launch. |

---

### 2. Informal Groups (The "Social" Network)

**Definition:** These are **not** created by management. They form naturally and spontaneously out of the personal, social, and psychological needs of employees. There is no official structure, no fixed rules, and no formal authority. They are based on friendship, shared interests, or common backgrounds.

#### Sub-types of Informal Groups:

| Sub-Type | Meaning | Glue that binds them | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Interest Group** | Formed by employees who share a common hobby, passion, or non-work interest. They come together to pursue a shared activity. | **Common activity** (Cricket, movies, music). | The office cricket team that plays every Sunday. A group of employees who meet weekly to discuss stock market investments. |
| **Friendship Group** | Formed by employees who genuinely like each other and share strong social bonds, trust, and emotional companionship. | **Social connection / Liking** (Shared history, similar age, or just "chemistry"). | The "lunch gang" that eats together daily, shares personal problems, and hangs out after work hours. The group of colleagues who gossip by the water cooler. |

---

### 3. Formal vs. Informal Groups (The Exam Comparison Table)

You *must* write this table if a question asks you to differentiate between them. It's easy marks!

| Basis | Formal Group | Informal Group |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Formation** | Deliberately planned and created by management. | Naturally formed by employees due to social needs. |
| **Structure** | Rigid, defined hierarchy (Pyramid shape). | Fluid, no fixed structure (Web-like). |
| **Authority** | Flows from the official position (Manager's designation). | Flows from personal qualities (Charisma, age, seniority, personality). |
| **Purpose** | To achieve organizational objectives (Profit, productivity). | To satisfy social, emotional, and psychological needs. |
| **Communication** | Formal channels (Meetings, official emails, reports). | Grapevine (Word of mouth, gossip, casual chit-chat). |
| **Leadership** | Official, appointed manager. | Unofficial, emergent leader (chosen by the group's members). |
| **Rules** | Written rules, SOPs, and strict policies. | Unwritten norms and traditions (e.g., "We never betray a teammate to the boss"). |
| **Lifespan** | Continues as long as the organization/department exists (or project lasts). | Continues as long as members share common interests/friendship. |

---

### 4. Reference Groups and In-Groups vs. Out-Groups (Bonus Advanced Concepts!)

To score *top-tier* marks and impress your examiner, throw in these two additional classifications used in organizational behavior:

#### A. Reference Group
- **Meaning:** A group that an individual looks up to and uses as a benchmark for their own attitudes, values, and behavior. The person may or may not actually *belong* to this group, but they *aspire* to be like them.
- **Example:** A junior executive might use the senior leadership team (the board of directors) as their *reference group*. They observe how the leaders dress, speak, and make decisions, and try to imitate them to get promoted.

#### B. In-Group vs. Out-Group
- **In-Group:** The group that an individual feels they *belong* to. They are "us." The individual shows loyalty, favoritism, and positive bias toward these members.
- **Out-Group:** The group that an individual feels they do *not* belong to. They are "them." The individual may feel competitive, indifferent, or hostile toward these members.
- **Example:** In an office, the members of the "Cricket Team" are the *In-Group*; they share inside jokes and go for drinks together. The people who don't play cricket are the *Out-Group* and might feel left out.

---

### The Golden Exam Conclusion Paragraph:

> *"Groups are the lifeblood of any organization. While **Formal groups** (Command and Task) provide the structural backbone needed to execute work efficiently, **Informal groups** (Interest and Friendship) provide the social glue that keeps employees emotionally connected and motivated. A smart manager recognizes that both are essential; they rely on formal groups for productivity, but carefully nurture informal groups because they boost morale, improve communication through the grapevine, and reduce employee turnover. Ignoring informal groups is a recipe for workplace alienation and high attrition."*
