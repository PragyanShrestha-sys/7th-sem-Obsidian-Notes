
These theories emerged as a reaction to the **Classical Theory**, which ignored the human element. They shifted the focus from **efficiency** and **structure** to **people**, **emotions**, **groups**, and **motivation**.

---

## Part 1: Human Relations Theory (1930s–1950s)

### What is it?
A school of thought that emphasizes the **social and psychological needs** of workers, arguing that satisfied workers are more productive. It focuses on **informal groups**, **communication**, and **leadership** rather than just rules and money.

### Key Foundations: The Hawthorne Studies (1924–1932)
Conducted at Western Electric's Hawthorne plant in Chicago. These studies revealed that social factors (attention, recognition, belonging) matter more than physical conditions (lighting, breaks).

| Experiment | Key Finding |
| --- | --- |
| Illumination Experiments | Productivity increased regardless of light level – due to being observed. |
| Relay Assembly Test Room | Productivity kept rising with any change (rest breaks, hours, pay) – because workers felt valued. |
| Bank Wiring Observation Room | Informal group norms restricted output more than formal rules. |

### Core Concepts of Human Relations Theory

1. **Hawthorne Effect** – People change their behavior when they know they are being studied/observed.
2. **Informal Organization** – Social groups, friendships, and grapevine communication exist alongside formal structure.
3. **Social Needs** – Workers need belonging, recognition, and respect – not just money.
4. **Group Norms** – Peer pressure can override financial incentives.
5. **Participative Supervision** – Managers who listen and involve workers get better results than autocratic ones.

### Key Contributors (Human Relations)

| Contributor | Contribution |
| --- | --- |
| **Elton Mayo** | Interpreted Hawthorne findings; argued that social factors drive productivity. |
| **Fritz Roethlisberger** | Studied informal organization and worker sentiments. |
| **William J. Dickson** | Documented the Hawthorne studies; emphasized counseling and communication. |

### Limitations of Human Relations Theory

| Limitation               | Explanation                                                              |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Overly optimistic        | Assumes happy workers are always productive – not always true.           |
| Ignores economic factors | Downplays the role of pay, job security, and working conditions.         |
| Manipulative potential   | "Making workers feel good" can be a tool for exploitation.               |
| Lacks scientific rigor   | Hawthorne studies had methodological flaws (small samples, no controls). |
| Underestimates conflict  | Assumes manager-worker harmony is easily achieved.                       |

## Part 2: Behavioural Science Theory (1950s–present)

### What is it?
A more **scientific and rigorous** extension of Human Relations. It applies psychology, sociology, anthropology, and economics to understand **individual and group behavior** in organizations. It moves beyond "happy workers are productive" to "what specifically motivates, satisfies, and influences people."

### Core Concepts of Behavioural Science Theory

1. **Motivation** – Internal drives that direct behavior toward goals.
2. **Leadership Styles** – Different approaches (autocratic, democratic, laissez-faire) suit different situations.
3. **Group Dynamics** – How groups form, function, and influence members.
4. **Organizational Culture** – Shared values, beliefs, and norms.
5. **Decision-Making** – Cognitive biases, bounded rationality, and groupthink.
6. **Personality and Attitudes** – Individual differences affect job performance.
7. **Job Design** – Enlargement, enrichment, and characteristics that make work meaningful.

### Key Contributors and Theories (Behavioural Science)

| Contributor            | Theory                            | Core Idea                                                                                                             |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Abraham Maslow**     | [[Hierarchy of Needs]]            | Needs in pyramid: Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualization. Lower needs must be satisfied first. |
| **Douglas McGregor**   | Theory X & Theory Y               | Theory X (workers are lazy, need coercion). Theory Y (workers are self-directed, seek responsibility).                |
| **Frederick Herzberg** | Two-Factor Theory                 | Hygiene factors (pay, conditions) prevent dissatisfaction. Motivators (achievement, recognition) create satisfaction. |
| **David McClelland**   | Three Needs Theory                | Need for Achievement (nAch), Power (nPow), and Affiliation (nAff) drive behavior.                                     |
| **Chris Argyris**      | Immaturity-Maturity Theory        | People develop from dependent to independent; rigid organizations block this growth.                                  |
| **Rensis Likert**      | System 1–4 Management             | System 1 (exploitative authoritative) to System 4 (participative group). System 4 is most effective.                  |
| **Kurt Lewin**         | Leadership Styles & Change Theory | Autocratic, democratic, laissez-faire. Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze model.                                            |
| **Blake & Mouton**     | Managerial Grid                   | Concern for people vs. concern for production. Team management (9,9) is ideal.                                        |
| **Victor Vroom**       | Expectancy Theory                 | Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence (Effort → Performance → Reward).                                  |
| **Edwin Locke**        | Goal-Setting Theory               | Specific, challenging goals with feedback lead to higher performance.                                                 |
| **Stacey Adams**       | Equity Theory                     | People compare their input/output ratio to others; perceived inequity causes distress.                                |
| **B.F. Skinner**       | Reinforcement Theory              | Behavior is shaped by consequences (positive reinforcement, punishment, extinction).                                  |

### Limitations of Behavioural Science Theory

| Limitation | Explanation |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Complexity | Human behavior is too complex for simple models; no single theory explains everything. |
| 2. Cultural bias | Most theories developed in Western (especially American) contexts – may not apply globally. |
| 3. Difficult to apply | Many concepts (e.g., self-actualization) are abstract and hard to measure or implement. |
| 4. Ignores structural factors | Overemphasizes psychology; downplays power, politics, and economic constraints. |
| 5. Contradictory findings | Different studies produce conflicting results (e.g., does satisfaction cause productivity or vice versa?). |
| 6. Manipulation risk | Can be used to "manage" employees' feelings without addressing real problems. |
| 7. Time and cost | Implementing behavioral approaches (training, surveys, participation) is expensive and slow. |
| 8. Not universally effective | Participation and enrichment don't work for all jobs or all people (some prefer structure). |
