To answer your question directly: **No, it is not impossible** for A to be a rival of C, even though A defers to B and B defers to C. 

In fact, status theory (particularly as developed by Joseph Berger and his colleagues) predicts exactly when and why this rivalry happens. 

Here is why it is possible, and exactly what status theory says about it:

**1. Status is relative, not absolute**
Status is not a single, fixed number that everyone agrees on. It is *locally* negotiated based on the specific people in a group. If A defers to B, and B defers to C, A *should* defer to C **only if** status is perfectly transitive (a straight hierarchy) and all actors are operating in the exact same social context. But humans don't work that way.

**2. The "Status Contradiction" (The key to your question)**
Status theory deals with **status characteristics** (e.g., education, age, race, gender, expertise) and **specific task abilities**. 

- If A defers to B because B is an expert in **Math**.
- And B defers to C because C is an expert in **Physics**.
- A might be a rival to C because A is an expert in **Physics**, too! 

In this case, A defers to B on Math, B defers to C on Physics, but A *does not* defer to C on Physics because A and C have equal or competing status in that specific domain. The deference is **domain-specific**. The transitive flow of status stops when you change the task.

**3. The "Inconsistency" Principle**
Status theory explicitly states that if A defers to B (meaning A sees B as higher status), and B defers to C (B sees C as higher status), but A refuses to defer to C (A sees C as a rival), this creates a **status inconsistency**. 

When this happens, the theory predicts that the group will experience **interactional tension**. The group will actively work to resolve this inconsistency through one of three pathways:

- **The "Burden of Proof" shifts:** The group will assume that since B (who is high status) defers to C, C *must* be high status. A will feel **social pressure** to defer to C. If A resists, A risks losing their own status because they are challenging the judgment of B.
- **A will "scope out" the situation:** A will search for a new dimension. A might think, *"Sure, B is higher status than me, and B says C is higher status... but I beat C in a competition last year."* A uses this outside evidence to justify maintaining rivalry with C, effectively rejecting B's judgment in this specific case.
- **The group "fractures":** If A stubbornly remains a rival to C despite B's deference, the group may split into subgroups. A and C will compete directly to prove who actually has higher status, and B will be forced to pick a side.

**4. The "Referential Structures" rule**
Finally, status theory says that for A's deference to B, and B's deference to C, to *force* A to defer to C, A must **personally believe** that the characteristic B and C share is a legitimate measure of status. If A thinks C is a fraud, or that C's expertise is outdated, the transitive logic breaks down. A will treat C as a rival because A rejects the "status value" that B assigns to C.

---

**To summarize:** Status theory does **not** say this situation is impossible. It says this situation is **unstable**. 

If A is a rival to C, but A defers to B, and B defers to C, then A is challenging B's judgment. The theory predicts that **A will eventually either:**

- Give in and defer to C (to avoid conflict with B), OR
- Challenge C directly and try to prove that B is wrong about C's status (escalating the rivalry).

If A continues to be a rival to C without challenging B, A will suffer a status loss, because the group will see A as irrational for ignoring the clear status chain.
