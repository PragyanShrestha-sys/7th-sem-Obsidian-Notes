## Step 3: Generate candidate 3-itemsets (C₃) ← **This is your main question**

### The Rule for k=3 (pairs → triplets):

> To generate a candidate triplet, take two **frequent pairs** that share their **first (k-2) = first 1 item**, then join them.

**Why this rule?**  
Because of the Apriori property: if a triplet is frequent, **all its pairs must be in L₂**. By joining only pairs that share the first item, we ensure the resulting triplet's first two pairs exist. Then we will prune if the third pair is missing.

---

### Step 3a: Sort L₂ lexicographically 

L₂ sorted:  
1. {jj, M}  
2. {C, J}  
3. {M, S}

note: "Lexicographic" ==relates to the creation of dictionaries or to an alphabetical (dictionary-style) order==

---

### Step 3b: Join pairs

We compare each pair with later pairs.

| Pair 1 | Pair 2 | First item same? | Action | Resulting triplet |
|--------|--------|------------------|--------|-------------------|
| {C,M} | {C,J} | Yes (C = C) | Join | {C, M, J} |
| {C,M} | {M,S} | No (C ≠ M) | Stop for {C,M} (no more share C) | — |
| {C,J} | {M,S} | No (C ≠ M) | No join | — |

**Only 1 candidate generated: C₃ = { {C, M, J} }**

---

### Step 3c: Pruning step (Apriori property)

For candidate {C, M, J}, check if **all its 2-item subsets** are in L₂:

| Subset | In L₂?                                     |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| {C, M} | ✅ Yes                                      |
| {C, J} | ✅ Yes                                      |
| {M, J} | ❌ No (was in C₂ but count=2, so not in L₂) |

**Since {M, J} is NOT frequent, {C, M, J} cannot be frequent.**  
We **prune** it — remove from C₃.

**After pruning: C₃ = ∅** (no candidates left)

---

### Step 3d: Scan database for remaining C₃

Since C₃ is empty, we skip scanning. Nothing to count.

**L₃ = ∅**

Algorithm stops.

---

## Why could we not generate {C, M, S} as a candidate?

Let's check if {C, M, S} could be generated by the join rule:

- To generate {C, M, S}, we would need two pairs that share first item:
  - {C, M} + {C, S} → but {C, S} is NOT in L₂ (count was only 2)
  - {C, M} + {M, S} → first item C vs M → not same, so cannot join

So {C, M, S} never even becomes a candidate. Apriori saves time by not generating it.

## Why This Join Rule? (The Heart of Your Question)

| Without Apriori | With Apriori |
|----------------|--------------|
| Generate all C(5,3)=10 triplets | Generate only from L₂ pairs |
| Check all 10 against 10 receipts | Generate 1 candidate |
| 100 checks total | Prune it without scanning |
| Fine for 5 items | Still fine here |

**But for 10,000 items:**
- Without Apriori: C(10000,3) ≈ 166 billion triplets → impossible
- With Apriori: If L₂ has only 1000 frequent pairs, candidates are few → feasible

## The Complete Generation Rule for Any k

| From | To | Join condition | Example |
|------|-----|----------------|---------|
| L₁ (singles) | C₂ (pairs) | All combinations | {C} + {M} = {C,M} |
| L₂ (pairs) | C₃ (triplets) | Share first 1 item | {C,M}+{C,J} = {C,M,J} |
| L₃ (triplets) | C₄ (4-itemsets) | Share first 2 items | {A,B,C}+{A,B,D} = {A,B,C,D} |
| Lₖ₋₁ | Cₖ | Share first k-2 items | General rule |

## Final Answer to Your Question

> How are triplets being made?

**Short answer:**  
By taking two **frequent pairs** that have the **same first item** and combining them:  
Pair1 = (X, Y), Pair2 = (X, Z) → Triplet = (X, Y, Z)

**Then prune** if (Y, Z) is not a frequent pair.

**Why not just count all triplets directly?**  
Because for real datasets with thousands of items, there are too many triplets to count. Apriori's join+prune strategy dramatically reduces the number of candidates you need to check.
