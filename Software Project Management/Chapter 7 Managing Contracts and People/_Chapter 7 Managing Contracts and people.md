
## Introduction 
A **contract** is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties (individuals, companies, or organizations) that creates mutual obligations enforceable by law. For a contract to be valid, it typically requires:

- **Offer** – One party proposes terms.
- **Acceptance** – The other party agrees to the terms.
- **Consideration** – Something of value is exchanged (e.g., money, services, goods).
- **Mutual intent** – Both parties intend to be legally bound.
- **Capacity and legality** – Parties are legally capable, and the purpose is lawful.
    

In **software project management**, contracts define the scope, deliverables, timelines, payment terms, intellectual property rights, confidentiality, warranties, liability, and dispute resolution mechanisms between the client and the software development team or vendor.

## [[Stages of contract placement]]

| Stage                   | Key Question                               |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Proposal Stage          | What is the vendor offering?               |
| Negotiation Stage       | Can we agree on price, scope, and risk?    |
| Contract Drafting Stage | Is everything written clearly and legally? |
| Review & Approval Stage | Do all internal stakeholders approve?      |
| Execution Stage         | Are signatures in place to begin work?     |
| Closure Stage           | Is everything delivered and accepted?      |

These six stages ensure that software contracts are placed systematically, reducing legal risk and misalignment before development begins.

## [[Contract Management]]

**Contract Management** is the process of actively managing a contract between a client and a software vendor from the point of execution (signing) through project delivery, payment, and final closure. Its goal is to ensure that both parties fulfill their agreed-upon obligations, deliverables, timelines, and quality standards while minimizing disputes and financial risk.

In software projects, contract management bridges the gap between the legal document and the daily technical work of building, testing, and deploying software.

## What is Decision Making?

**Decision Making** is the process of:
- **Identifying a problem**
- **Evaluating alternatives**
- **Selecting the best course of action**

In software projects, decisions range from simple daily choices (e.g., which task to prioritize) to complex strategic judgments (e.g., which architecture to use for a new system).

There are two types of Decision in SPM

| Feature              | Structured Decisions           | Unstructured Decisions                      |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Nature**           | Routine, repetitive            | Unique, non-routine                         |
| **Rules**            | Predefined, clear              | No clear rules                              |
| **Uncertainty**      | Low                            | High                                        |
| **Approach**         | Follow procedure               | Judgment, creativity, experience            |
| **Automation**       | Easy to automate               | Difficult or impossible to automate         |
| **Software Example** | Assigning a daily standup time | Choosing a cloud provider for a new product |

## [[Leadership and Leadership Styles]]

While the image focuses on leadership styles, leadership itself can be defined as:

**Leadership** is the ability to influence, motivate, and enable others to contribute toward the effectiveness and success of an organization or project.

In software project management, leadership goes beyond managing tasks—it involves guiding the team through technical challenges, resolving conflicts, and maintaining morale during setbacks.
