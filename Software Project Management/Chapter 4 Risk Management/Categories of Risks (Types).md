Based on the categories listed in your image—**Project Risks**, **Technical Risks**, and **Business Risks**—here is a clear explanation of each type of risk in software project management.

---

## 1. Project Risks

**Definition:** Risks that affect the **management** and **execution** of the project itself—typically related to schedule, resources, budget, and organization.

These are often within the project manager’s direct control or influence.

### Common Examples:
- **Schedule risks:** Unrealistic deadlines, poor task estimation, dependency delays.
- **Resource risks:** Key team member leaves, insufficient staff, budget cuts.
- **Management risks:** Poor communication, unclear roles, weak leadership.
- **Process risks:** Inefficient development methodology, lack of documentation.

### Typical Impact:
- Missed milestones
- Cost overruns
- Team burnout

> **Example:** *"The client requests major changes two weeks before launch, but no change management process is in place."*

---

## 2. Technical Risks

**Definition:** Risks related to the **technology, tools, architecture, and implementation** of the software.

These are usually handled by the development team and technical leads.

### Common Examples:
- **Technology choice:** Using an unfamiliar or immature framework/library.
- **Integration issues:** Third-party APIs change or fail; legacy systems don't connect.
- **Performance risks:** System becomes too slow under expected user load.
- **Quality risks:** Poor code quality leads to bugs, security vulnerabilities, or technical debt.
- **Tooling risks:** Build system fails, version control issues, test automation breaks.

### Typical Impact:
- Low-quality software
- Delays from rework
- Security breaches

> **Example:** *"The chosen database cannot handle the projected data volume, forcing a costly migration mid-project."*

---

## 3. Business Risks

**Definition:** Risks that affect the **value, viability, or market success** of the software product from an organizational perspective.

These are usually owned by product owners, stakeholders, or executives.

### Common Examples:
- **Market risks:** Building a feature nobody wants; competitor releases a better product first.
- **Requirement risks:** Unclear or constantly changing requirements from stakeholders.
- **Legal/regulatory risks:** New data privacy law (e.g., GDPR) invalidates current design.
- **Vendor risks:** Critical third-party service goes out of business or raises prices.
- **Budget risks:** Funding is cut before the project is complete.

### Typical Impact:
- Project cancellation
- No return on investment (ROI)
- Legal penalties

> **Example:** *"The target audience shifts their preferences during the 6-month development cycle, making the product obsolete at launch."*

## Comparison Summary Table

| Category | Focus | Who Owns It? | Example |
|----------|-------|--------------|---------|
| **Project Risks** | Management & delivery | Project Manager | Key developer resigns |
| **Technical Risks** | Technology & code | Tech Lead / Team | Database can't scale |
| **Business Risks** | Value & market success | Product Owner / Stakeholders | Competitor launches first |

---

## Why This Categorization Matters

By grouping risks into these three types, you can:
- **Assign ownership** clearly (PM vs. Tech Lead vs. Product Owner).
- **Choose the right response strategy** (e.g., technical risk → mitigation via prototyping; business risk → acceptance with contingency).
- **Communicate effectively** with different stakeholders (executives care most about business risks).

> **Note:** Some frameworks add more categories (e.g., *External Risks* like weather or politics), but Project, Technical, and Business cover the vast majority of software project risks.
