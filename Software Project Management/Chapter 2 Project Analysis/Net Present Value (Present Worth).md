Net Present Value (NPV) or Present Worth measures the profitability of an investment by calculating the difference between the present value of cash inflows and outflows over time. It accounts for the time value of money, discounting future cash flows to today's value to determine if a project adds value.

### What is NPV?

**Net Present Value (NPV)** is a tool that tells you if an investment will make you richer.
It does this by recognizing that **money today is worth more than money tomorrow** (the Time Value of Money).

### The Core Idea

- **Positive NPV:** The project creates value. **Accept it.**
- **Negative NPV:** The project destroys value. **Reject it.**

### The Discount Rate (Your "Insurance Number")

The discount rate is the **hurdle rate** a project must clear. It represents three things:

1. **Time Value:** You need compensation for waiting for your money.
2. **Risk:** If there is a chance "things might go wrong," you demand a higher return to take that gamble.
3. **Opportunity Cost:** It is the return you would get by investing in the next best alternative (like paying off debt or buying government bonds).

Think of it like an **insurance number**:

- **Low risk** (calm lake) = Low discount rate (easy to get a positive NPV).
- **High risk** (stormy sea) = High discount rate (hard to get a positive NPV; the future cash flows must be massive to be worth it).

### Real-World Application

A company uses NPV to decide whether to buy a machine or build a factory. They estimate the future cash flows, discount them back to today using their **cost of capital** (the weighted average of what they pay for loans and investor money), and only move forward if the result is positive.

In short: **NPV answers "Will this investment increase our wealth today?" and the discount rate ensures we are properly paid for the risk and the wait.**
