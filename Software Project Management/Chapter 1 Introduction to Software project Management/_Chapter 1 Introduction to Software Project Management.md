1. Introduction
Software Project Management is ==the structured process of planning, organizing, scheduling, budgeting, and controlling software development to deliver high-quality, functional software on time and within budget==. It involves managing risks, resources, and stakeholders throughout the software development life cycle (SDLC)

---
[[Problems in Software Project Management]]

Software engineering problems in project management are ==core challenges arising from the technical complexity, uncertainty, and constant change inherent in developing software, often causing projects to exceed budgets, miss deadlines, and produce low-quality products==. Key issues include poor communication, scope creep, unrealistic stakeholder expectations, and difficulty managing technical debt.

---
[[Software product]]
A software product is ==a developed, maintained, and delivered software system designed to satisfy user needs, typically aimed at a specific market or customer==. It is the tangible output of a project—including code, documentation, and user support—that delivers value and often acts as a commercial, standalone solution.

---
[[Software Project ]]
A software project is ==a temporary, structured work to develop, maintain, or implement software products within specific constraints of time, budget, and scope==. It involves specialized activities—eliciting requirements, design, coding, testing, and maintenance—distinct from routine operations, aiming to deliver specific functionality to users

---
[[Difference between software project and other types of project]]

Software projects differ from traditional engineering or construction projects primarily due to their ==**invisibility**, high **complexity**, and extreme **flexibility**==. Unlike physical projects with tangible progress, software is logical, making it harder to track progress. They are heavily iterative, focusing on evolving requirements and intellectual output rather than linear manufacturing. 

---
[[Activities covered by SPM ]]
Software project management covers the entire lifecycle to ensure successful delivery within constraints, focusing on three key stages: ==feasibility studies (assessing viability), planning (defining scope, budget, and schedule), and execution (implementation and monitoring)==. These activities ensure cost-benefit analysis, risk management, and quality control, using tools like Work Breakdown Structures (WBS) to manage resources effectively.
