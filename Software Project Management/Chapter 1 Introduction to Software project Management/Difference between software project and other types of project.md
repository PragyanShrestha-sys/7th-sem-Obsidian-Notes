Software projects differ from traditional engineering or construction projects primarily due to their ==**invisibility**, high **complexity**, and extreme **flexibility**==. Unlike physical projects with tangible progress, software is logical, making it harder to track progress. They are heavily iterative, focusing on evolving requirements and intellectual output rather than linear manufacturing. 

Key differences between software projects and other project types include:

- **Invisibility of Progress:** In physical projects (e.g., building a bridge), progress is visible. In software, the product is intangible until final delivery, making it difficult to measure intermediate progress.
- **Flexibility and Change:** Software is highly flexible and easy to modify, even late in development, whereas changing physical structures is costly or impossible.
- **High Complexity and Uncertainty:** Software systems often possess more complex interdependencies than physical structures, with full complexity often revealed only during implementation.
- **Iterative Process:** Software development involves continuous testing, debugging, and refinement (often Agile), rather than the linear "design-build-test" phases of traditional projects.
- **Uncertainty in Requirements:** Software requirements are rarely fully known at the start, leading to high potential for scope creep compared to more static project types.
- **Labor Intensive:** Software development is predominantly a cognitive activity, focusing on creating knowledge, designs, and code, rather than using physical materials.
